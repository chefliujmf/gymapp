# Time to Exhaustion (TTE) — coach knowledge base

Source: CTS / TrainRight — *Understanding Time to Exhaustion (TTE) and How You Can Improve This Metric*
(https://trainright.com/understanding-time-to-exhaustion-tte-and-how-you-can-improve-this-metric/).
Added 2026-07-07 at JM's request — "good for the coach". Platyplus surfaces TTE as a learned benchmark per
sport (#401); this is the theory the coach should reason from.

## Definition
**TTE = the maximum duration a power equal to FTP (or a pace equal to threshold) can be maintained** before
fatigue forces output down. It reframes threshold as a **power–duration pair**, not a single number:
> "FTP isn't only a number… FTP can be better determined as a power duration." — e.g. *200 W for 35 min*, not just *200 W*.

## Typical range
Athletes hold FTP/threshold for roughly **30–70 minutes**, by training status:
- **Moderately trained:** ~30–40 min TTE
- **Well-trained:** ~45–75 min TTE

## Interpreting a SHORT TTE (important — the nuanced view)
A short TTE **does not automatically mean the FTP/threshold is set wrong** — it reveals *current physiological
capacity*. An athlete with a 200 W FTP but only a 35-min TTE has a legitimate target: **extend the duration
through threshold work, not immediately chase higher watts.**
- BUT if TTE is **far below** the 30-min floor (e.g. only a few minutes at FTP), the reference is likely a touch
  HIGH — cross-check against eFTP / critical power. In Platyplus terms: if TTE at the *manual* FTP is tiny but
  the *eFTP* would give a 30–70-min TTE, nudge the athlete toward the eFTP.
- Running: the same logic — a short TTE at the stated threshold pace usually means the threshold pace is set too
  fast (faster than critical speed).

## How to IMPROVE TTE — "longer durations at threshold, not more power"
**Extensive threshold training** (the primary method), at **90–95% of FTP** (sub-threshold so it's sustainable):
- 3 × 15–20 min · 3 × 24 min · 4 × 15 min · up to 1 × 60 min continuous
- Recovery **6–10 min** between intervals
- **Total work time ≈ the athlete's current TTE or slightly under** — enough to overload without burying them.
Plus **aerobic volume** to lay the groundwork for a higher future FTP (for athletes plateauing on FTP gains).

## Raising FTP (vs extending TTE) — pick by the athlete's event
| Athlete / event | Focus | Sample workout | Power |
|---|---|---|---|
| Beginner / moderate | Build FTP | 3–4 × 8 min | 100–105% FTP |
| Well-trained | Raise FTP | 4 × 10–12 min | 100–105% FTP |
| Road / gravel (2–6 h events) | **Extend TTE** | 3 × 20–24 min | 90–95% FTP |
| Crit / XC MTB (30–90 min) | **Maximize FTP** | intensive intervals | 100–105% FTP |

**Rule of thumb:** long events reward a long TTE (fatigue resistance); short/punchy events reward raw FTP power.

## How the coach should use this
- When TTE is short, **default to prescribing extensive threshold work** (the above) to extend it — *not* "your
  FTP is wrong". Only flag FTP-too-high when TTE is implausibly short vs eFTP/CP.
- Match the emphasis to the athlete's **goal event** (long → TTE, short → FTP).
- Keep threshold-interval **total work time near their current TTE**.
- TTE is a **power–duration pair** — coach the pair, not the watt alone.
