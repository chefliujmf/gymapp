# ROAR — Female-Athlete Physiology: Coach Source Notes

> **APPLICABILITY:** This file captures **female-athlete** physiology. It is knowledge for **future FEMALE athletes** of the engine. It does **NOT apply to Jean-Manuel (male)** — do not use any cycle-phase, female-hormone, or female-specific fueling guidance for JMF. Keep it in the engine because the coach is multi-tenant and will eventually coach women.

> **Source / inspiration:** *ROAR: How to Match Your Food and Fitness to Your Unique Female Physiology* — Stacy T. Sims, PhD, with Selene Yeager (Rodale, 2016). Paraphrased for coaching use; no passages or tables transcribed. Numbers below are the book's stated figures, recorded as starting points to individualize — not as fixed law.

> **READ THIS FIRST — CONTESTED SCIENCE:** Sims's thesis is "women are not small men." Several of her strong/absolute claims (precise cycle-phase fueling shifts, "ice baths help women but not men," fructose-malabsorption framing, blanket anti-Pill stance, exact phase-by-phase performance effects) go **beyond settled evidence**; the menstrual-cycle-and-performance literature is heterogeneous and many effects are small or individual. **Treat the cycle-specific and sex-difference claims as HYPOTHESES to apply judiciously and individualize, not dogma.** Items below tagged **[CONTESTED]** are the ones to soft-pedal. The robust, broadly-agreed material is: eat enough (avoid low energy availability/RED-S), prioritize protein and recovery fueling, lift heavy, protect bone and iron.

## 1. Menstrual-cycle-aware training & fueling

**What it covers:** How the two cycle phases shift fueling, thermoregulation, fluid balance, muscle metabolism, mood, and how to plan around them.

**Phase model (book's framing):**
- **Low-hormone phase** = menstruation + early follicular (roughly day 1 through ovulation, ~day 14). Estrogen/progesterone low. Sims: physiology is "most like a man's" here — better carb access, stronger, recovers faster, tolerates heat better.
- **High-hormone phase** = luteal (post-ovulation to next period, ~day 15–28). Estrogen and progesterone high, peaking ~5 days pre-menstruation (PMS window).
- Cycle length varies 21–35 days; not everyone is a clean 28. **Track each athlete's own cycle** rather than assuming day numbers.

**Coach-owned principles + prescriptions:**
- **Schedule the hardest strength / high-intensity / PR attempts in the low-hormone phase** when feasible. The book cites a 20-woman study of greater strength gains training in the low-hormone phase. **[CONTESTED — apply as a soft preference, confirm against the athlete's own logged RPE/performance.]**
- **Don't deload purely because a key event lands in the luteal/PMS window.** Sims notes VO2max and lactate threshold stay roughly constant across the cycle, so endurance PRs are still achievable with PMS (Paula Radcliffe set the marathon WR with cramps). Skill/reaction-heavy sports may dip slightly — not very relevant to steady-state cycling.
- **Fuel MORE carbohydrate in the high-hormone/luteal phase**, especially around long/intense sessions: estrogen blunts carb-burning and raises fat use, so high-intensity work needs replenished carbs. The book also notes a ~5–10% metabolic uptick (~100–200 extra kcal/day) premenstrually. **[CONTESTED magnitude — use as "lean toward a bit more carb and total intake premenstrually," watch the athlete's energy/hunger.]**
- **Protein/leucine emphasis is higher in the high-hormone phase** because estrogen down-regulates muscle anabolism and progesterone up-regulates catabolism → faster muscle breakdown during hard efforts. Push pre- and post-exercise leucine-rich protein (see §2).
- **Heat & fluids in the high-hormone phase:** progesterone raises core temp, plasma volume can drop up to ~8%, and more sodium is shed → hotter, harder to sweat/cool, higher hyponatremia risk. **Pre-hydrate/sodium-preload before luteal-phase sessions in heat** (see §3).

**How cycle phases adjust the plan (practical summary):**
- Low-hormone phase: green-light heavy lifting, intervals, PR efforts; standard fueling.
- High-hormone/PMS phase: keep training load but **add carbs around hard work, add protein/leucine, pre-load fluids+sodium, expect higher RPE.** Don't read elevated RPE/HR as lost fitness.

**Symptom-mitigation toolkit the book gives (offer, don't prescribe medically):**
- Cramps/GI: in the 5–7 days pre-period — magnesium, omega-3s, and low-dose (80 mg) aspirin (book specifies aspirin over ibuprofen because it suppresses prostaglandins irreversibly). **Caution: medication advice — defer to the athlete's physician; coach only flags the pattern.**
- Menstrual headaches/migraines (estrogen drop just before flow): stay hydrated; nitric-oxide-rich foods (beets, pomegranate, watermelon, spinach) in the lead-up.
- CNS fatigue/low mood/"lost mojo": BCAAs/leucine may blunt serotonin-driven central fatigue.

**Cycle manipulation / contraceptives [CONTESTED — opinion-heavy]:** Sims discourages using the Pill or synthetic progesterone to time races (cites muscle/strength downsides and a study of reduced muscle-mass gains on the Pill); prefers natural symptom management or localized hormone methods. Present as one view; this is the athlete's and her doctor's call, not the coach's.

## 2. Female-specific fueling — carbohydrate, protein, fat, timing

**What it covers:** Daily macro targets and pre/during/post-exercise fueling tuned to female physiology.

**Daily macro split (book's working target):** ~40% carb / ~30% protein / ~30% fat (whole-food carbs ~40–45%). Sims argues women become more carb-sensitive with age (declining estrogen) so favor low-glycemic, nutrient-dense carbs (starchy/root veg, fruit, ancient grains) over refined starch.

**Carbohydrate needs (per lb body weight per day):**
- Light / rest / short-intense (e.g. CrossFit) day: **1.13–1.4 g/lb**
- Moderate-high intensity 60–120 min: **1.6–1.8 g/lb**
- Endurance 2–5 h/day (cycling, running, swim): **2.0–2.7 g/lb**
- Extreme 5+ h/day (Ironman/multisport): **2.7–3.1 g/lb**
- Absolute survival floor (non-training): **~130 g/day** — never program below this.
- Anti-low-carb stance: Sims argues very-low-carb/keto is worse for women — raises cortisol, harms protein synthesis and immunity. **[CONTESTED for individuals, but the "don't chronically under-carb female athletes" message is sound.]**

**Protein (the book's headline female adjustment — RDA is too low):**
- Daily target: **~1.0–1.2 g/lb/day** (≈2.2–2.6 g/kg) for active women; high end if training hard. (Note: this is high vs. mainstream sports-nutrition ~1.6–2.2 g/kg — flag as Sims's upper-end recommendation.)
- **Front-load:** 25–30 g protein at the morning meal (counters overnight catabolic state).
- **Leucine matters most:** whey is leucine-rich; ~25 g whey ≈ leucine of ~50 g soy. For injury/heavy-load recovery: ~30 g protein with ~3 g leucine, ~3×/day.
- **Bedtime casein** snack to feed muscle repair overnight.

**Fat:** don't fear it — ~30% of intake; nuts/seeds/fish for quality fats.

**Workout fueling timing (book's protocol):**
- **0–1 h before (sessions >90 min):** ~10–15 g protein + ~40 g carb (~200–220 kcal), low-fiber, low-fat real food. The protein-before piece is the female-specific tweak (protect muscle vs. high-hormone catabolism).
- **During (efforts >60–90 min):** ~40–50 g carb/hour, **real food not pure gels** ("food in your pocket, hydration in your bottle"). Avoid fructose-heavy and maltodextrin-heavy products (GI distress, malabsorption).
- **After — recovery window:** restock **protein within 30 min** (25–30 g animal, or 20–25 g whey+casein, or ~50 g soy / ~48 g rice); restock **carbs within 2 h** (glucose-rich). A later 20–25 g protein top-up 2–3 h post is used in her taper plans.
- **Separate fuel from fluid:** calories from food, hydration from a properly formulated drink — don't use the bottle as a calorie vehicle.

**Postmenopausal note [CONTESTED specifics]:** Sims claims postmenopausal women metabolize fructose poorly → push them off fructose-based gels/drinks toward low-fructose real food, raise protein (~120–130 g/day in her case study), add power work. The clinical fructose mechanism is debated; the actionable, safe takeaway is: **older female athletes still need female-specific fueling — adequate protein, low-GI carbs, real-food fueling, don't assume "post-menopause = train like anyone."**

## 3. Hydration & heat differences

**What it covers:** Why female fluid balance differs and how to formulate/time drinks.

**Female-specific drivers:** estrogen/progesterone shift plasma volume (down to ~8% lower in high-hormone phase), core temp (progesterone raises it, delays sweat), and sodium handling (more sodium shed) → women are **more prone to hyponatremia** and have a less reliable thirst signal, especially in the luteal phase.

**Coach-owned principles + prescriptions:**
- **Drink to a plan, not purely to thirst** for female athletes in long/hot efforts (thirst science was largely derived from men), but **never over-drink plain water** — hyponatremia is a real risk and hit more women in the cited Boston Marathon data.
- **Hydration drink formula (per 8 oz):** ~3–4% carb (7–9.4 g), glucose+sucrose (not fructose/maltodextrin), **sodium ~180–225 mg**, potassium ~60–75 mg. (≈ 350–450 mg sodium per 16 oz.) Sodium+glucose co-transport is what actually pulls fluid in; plain water and 5–8% carb "fuel" drinks hydrate poorly.
- **No salt tablets** — they can cause GI distress and reverse water flux; sodium needs come from a correctly formulated drink plus diet.
- **Pre-load before heat / luteal-phase hot sessions:** night-before high-sodium broth; a sodium pre-load drink (book's DIY: ~7.7 g sodium citrate + 4.5 g sodium chloride per liter; or ~0.15 oz/lb body weight of a hyperhydration drink ~20 min pre-start). **[CONTESTED precision — use as "prioritize sodium pre-loading," individualize doses.]**
- **Heat thresholds:** performance falls apart as core climbs; muscles falter ~102°F, CNS alarms ~104–105.8°F. Schedule hot sessions for cooler hours.

## 4. Strength training emphasis

**What it covers:** Why heavy lifting is non-negotiable for women and the dose.

**Rationale:** women start with less muscle and lose it faster (~3%/decade muscle mass 30→80; strength −30% from 50→70) because estrogen blunts anabolism, progesterone raises catabolism. Cardio alone does not preserve lean mass past ~40.

**Coach-owned principles + prescriptions:**
- **Lift HEAVY for strength/power, not light "toning."** Weight selection cue: last 1–2 of ~10 reps are barely completable with good form. Body-weight (pushups, single-leg/pistol squats) counts if it reaches the same fatigue.
- **Dose:** 2–3 sets × ~10 reps; **2–3 days/week (3 preferable).**
- **Include power/plyometrics** (jumps) for type-II fiber recruitment and bone — except where bone density is compromised (see §5).
- **Rotate exercises every 2–3 weeks** to avoid plateau.
- **Brace core / neutral spine** on lifts.
- **Progressions:** body-weight → loaded → heavy/power + plyometric; for masters women, "add power-based work" to slow age-related body-composition change.

## 5. Bone health

**What it covers:** Female osteoporosis risk and how training/diet protect bone.

**Rationale:** women = ~80% of US osteoporosis cases; lower peak bone mass than men; loss ~0.5–1%/yr premenopause, accelerating to ~2–3%/yr post-menopause as estrogen drops.

**Coach-owned principles + prescriptions:**
- **Cycling and swimming are NOT bone-building** (non-weight-bearing). For predominantly-cycling female athletes, **add weight-bearing/impact cross-training**: running, jumping/plyometrics, dancing, ball sports, **plus strength training** — especially targeting upper body/torso.
- Strength training 2–3×/week can add ~1%/yr bone density at spine/hip even in low-density postmenopausal women.
- **Posture/upper-back work** (between shoulder blades) to resist kyphosis/compression fractures; **tai chi/balance** to cut fall risk.
- **If osteopenia/osteoporosis diagnosed:** avoid high-impact plyometrics and heavy spinal flexion/twisting; keep lifting but choose moves carefully.
- **Calcium/vitamin D [CONTESTED]:** Sims is skeptical of high-dose dairy/calcium supplementation; favors food sources (sardines, salmon — ~200–300 mg Ca/serving, salmon high in D), fortified yogurt, and a vitamin D supplement. Mainstream IOM targets she cites: 1,000 mg/day Ca (19–50 yr), 1,200 mg/day (>50). Bone scan (DXA) generally not needed until 65+ unless risk factors (family history, steroids, recurrent stress fractures).

## 6. Iron health

**What it covers:** Why female athletes are iron-depletion-prone and how to screen.

**Rationale:** premenopausal women lose iron via menses; athletes lose extra via foot-strike/red-cell breakdown, sweat, GI bleeding (impact, NSAIDs), and inflammation-driven malabsorption. Low iron → fatigue, poor workouts, feeling cold, irritability; correcting deficiency can raise max power and efficiency (more power at lower HR).

**Coach-owned principles + prescriptions:**
- **Need ~18 mg/day (women 19–50).** Food first: meats, nuts, grains; pair with vitamin C, separate from coffee/tea/calcium.
- **Screen ferritin/iron via blood test** if an athlete is fatigued, losing snap in workouts, or training/racing seriously — can be low without symptoms.
- **Don't blanket-supplement:** GI side effects; rare hemochromatosis risk; **postmenopausal women generally should NOT take iron** without testing. Supplementation = medical decision.
- **Coach action:** if performance fades + fatigue + heavy periods cluster, flag possible iron deficiency and recommend a blood panel; do not prescribe doses.

## 7. Low Energy Availability / RED-S — red flags, screening, prevention

**What it covers:** The biggest safety item. Reframes the old "female athlete triad" as **RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport)** per the 2014 IOC statement — driven primarily by **inadequate fueling for the training load**, affecting metabolism, menstrual function, bone, immunity, protein synthesis, cardiovascular and psychological health (and it affects men too).

**Key insight:** menstrual dysfunction is NOT limited to very lean / very-high-volume athletes — even women at 25–30% body fat can lose cycles if chronically under-fueled (a hypocaloric + high-stress state shuts down reproduction). Current low-carb/IF trends raise risk. The root cause to fix is **energy intake vs. expenditure**, not body fat per se.

**RED-S RED FLAGS (screen continuously):**
- **Missed/irregular/absent periods** (amenorrhea) — primary signal; ask female athletes about cycle regularity.
- **Stress fractures / recurrent bone injury** (e.g., the book's triathlete case with fibula/tibia stress injuries).
- **Rapid weight loss** (~5–10% body mass in a month), abnormally low body fat.
- **Chronic fatigue, stalled/declining performance, frequent illness, poor recovery, feeling cold, low mood.**
- **Disordered-eating patterns / food restriction / fear of weight gain**, low-carb + high-fiber eating that leaves the athlete full but under-fueled.
- Severe end = anorexia/serious eating disorders, dehydration-induced instability → **HIGH-RISK / medical referral**.

**Risk-stratify (book uses the IOC RED-S red/yellow/green model):**
- **Red (high):** eating disorders, prolonged very-low body fat (DXA), rapid weight loss, life-threatening signs → **stop/limit competition, medical clearance required.**
- **Yellow (moderate):** menstrual dysfunction, declining bone density, sub-optimal fueling → **intervene: increase intake, monitor, involve professionals.**
- **Green (low):** regular cycles, healthy bone, adequate energy → proceed.

**Prevention / coach-owned actions:**
- **Default to "fuel the work."** Match carbohydrate/total energy to training load (use §2 carb tables). Never let load climb while intake stays flat.
- **Protect the recovery window** (protein <30 min, carb <2 h) and daily protein.
- **Make cycle regularity a tracked health metric** for female athletes; treat a lost period as a load/fueling alarm, not a convenience.
- **Refer out:** suspected RED-S, eating disorder, or amenorrhea → physician / sports dietitian / psychologist. The coach **flags and de-loads; it does not diagnose or treat.**
- Bone loss from RED-S amenorrhea can be **partly irreversible** — act early.

## 8. Recovery (female-specific notes)

- **Always cool down** — women have a greater post-exercise drop in arterial blood pressure and off-load heat more slowly (progesterone keeps core warm, delays sweat). Skipping the cooldown limits nutrient delivery to muscles.
- **Cold-water immersion / ice baths [CONTESTED]:** Sims claims they help women (assist post-exercise vasoconstriction women lack) even if they may blunt adaptation in men. Treat as a hypothesis; note current evidence generally cautions that routine cold immersion can blunt strength/hypertrophy adaptation regardless of sex — use sparingly, not after key strength sessions.
- **Active recovery** (gentle movement next day) to keep blood moving.
- **Non-negotiables:** quality sleep + ≥1 full rest day/week; adequate carbs/protein (don't let "clean eating" turn into under-fueling — ties back to RED-S).
