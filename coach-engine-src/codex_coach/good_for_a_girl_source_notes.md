# Good for a Girl — Coach-Owned Source Notes

> **Scope & disclaimer.** This is coaching *philosophy and context* for developing and coaching **female athletes**, distilled from a memoir. It is for the engine's awareness when it coaches current/future female athletes. **It does NOT apply to Jean-Manuel (JMF), and it is NOT a training protocol or clinical guidance.** Nothing here is a diagnosis tool. When in doubt, the coach refers the athlete to qualified professionals (physician, sports dietitian, mental-health clinician) and never diagnoses.

## Source
- **Book:** *Good for a Girl: A Woman Running in a Man's World* — Lauren Fleshman (Penguin, 2023).
- **Type:** Memoir / narrative by an elite American distance runner turned coach. Cited as **inspiration only** — principles paraphrased, no passages transcribed.
- **Lens for these notes:** What a coach should internalize about the female athlete experience, long-term development, and harm avoidance.

## What it is
A first-person account of growing up and competing in a sport system built on male development norms. The throughline: girls and women were measured by, fueled by, and coached by models designed for boys and men — and the costs were injuries, lost health, eroded confidence, and disordered relationships with food and body. The author later becomes a coach and reframes the athlete as a whole person developing over decades, not a body to be made lean for the next season.

## Relevance (female-athlete coaching philosophy — not JMF, not a protocol)
- Female physiology is **not a smaller/weaker version of male physiology**; coaching that copies male templates can actively harm girls and women.
- Puberty changes (added fat, hips, breasts, a temporary performance plateau or dip) are **normal, healthy, and developmentally necessary** — not "nonproductive weight" to be trained or starved away.
- The years where short-term leanness produces fast results are often the same years when restriction does the most lasting damage to bone density and endocrine health. **Peak teenage performance is not the goal; a durable adult athlete is.**

## Coach-owned principles / lessons
- **Build long-term, healthy athletes — not short-term lean ones.** Resist the trade of future health and bone density for this season's numbers. The fast-growing-girl who is "ahead" at 15 is frequently not the one still thriving at 25.
- **Energy availability first.** Under-fueling relative to training load (low energy availability) is the root mechanism behind RED-S — disrupted hormones, weakened bones, stalled progress. Fuel adequately *for* the training; food is not a lever to shrink the athlete.
- **Treat the menstrual cycle as a vital sign.** A regular cycle is a marker of adequate energy and endocrine health. Loss or irregularity of periods is a warning the system is under-resourced — never a "perk" of being fit or a sign of dedication. (The book notes old "a few missed periods is fine" thresholds are outdated; any disruption deserves attention from a clinician.)
- **Watch language and framing.** Avoid weight talk, "ideal race weight," body-composition targets, and myths like "if you're fit, nothing jiggles." Coaching language about bodies sticks for life. Frame food as fuel and the body as something to work *with*, not against.
- **Respect puberty and adolescent development.** Expect and normalize the pubertal dip; protect the athlete's morale and identity through it instead of treating it as a problem to fix. Drive identity and motivation beyond the scale and the stopwatch.
- **Beware leanness culture and "gray area" permission.** Vague endorsement of "all elite athletes restrict a little" reads as a green light for disordered eating. Coaches set the norms; ambient leanness pressure plus silence equals harm. The book shows even a supportive coach can leave leanness pressure unchallenged — coaches must actively counter it.
- **Don't outsource the athlete's body awareness to logs and numbers.** Calorie tracking and rigid meal plans can disconnect an athlete from hunger and intuition; over-control is itself a red flag, not discipline.
- **Coach the whole person.** Performance is open-ended and athlete-owned; confidence, autonomy, and self-worth that don't depend on a body or a result are protective and performance-enhancing.

## Red flags + how the coach should respond
**Possible signs (non-diagnostic):** loss or irregularity of menstrual periods; recurring bone-stress injuries / stress fractures; preoccupation with food rules, labels, or calorie counts; "food pushing" or rituals to avoid eating; fear of normal eating; restricting fat or whole food groups; rapid or sought-after weight loss; fatigue, poor recovery, low mood, performance plateau despite training.

**How the coach responds:**
- **Refer out, never diagnose.** Direct the athlete to a physician, a registered sports dietitian, and/or a mental-health professional. RED-S and disordered eating are clinical matters.
- **Use supportive, non-judgmental, non-body-focused language.** Express care for the person and their long-term health, not their weight or performance.
- **Adjust the controllables.** Reinforce adequate fueling, reduce load if injuries recur, and remove leanness pressure from the program's culture and messaging.
- **Treat menstrual changes and repeat bone injuries as signals to escalate**, not to push through.
- **Never present restriction, missed periods, or leanness as acceptable trade-offs** for performance.

## Cautions
- This is **one athlete's lived experience and opinion**, not a clinical reference or controlled evidence. Use it to shape philosophy and awareness, not to make medical calls.
- It centers distance **running** in a US collegiate/elite context; the *mechanisms* (energy availability, menstrual health, leanness pressure) carry over to cycling, but specifics do not transfer 1:1.
- Some thresholds named in the book are explicitly flagged as outdated — defer to current clinical guidance and qualified professionals.
- **Applies to female athletes only; not to JMF and not a training plan.** When uncertain, refer out.
