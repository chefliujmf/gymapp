# Functional Training for Sports (Boyle) — Coach Source Notes

Offline-resilient, prescribable summary distilled from a strength reference. Original wording throughout; the book is cited as inspiration, not transcribed. Numbers below are coach defaults adapted for a cyclist; cross-check against `instructions_strength.md`.

## Source

- Title: New Functional Training for Sports, 2nd edition
- Author: Michael Boyle
- Year: 2016 (Human Kinetics)
- Format: book (.mobi in `knowledge_base/`)
- Type: book inspiration (strength & movement-quality reference)

## What it covers / scope

- Defining "functional" training (purposeful, sport-specific, movement-pattern based).
- Assessing functional strength; designing a program (patterns, frequency, progression).
- Foam rolling, stretching, and dynamic warm-up.
- Lower body (heavy bias to unilateral squat/hinge), core (anti-movement model), upper body, plyometrics, and conditioning philosophy.
- Strong opinions: train movements not muscles; the core is a stabilizer; unilateral work beats bilateral for most athletes.

## Relevance to a cyclist (Jean-Manuel and any athlete)

Cycling is a seated, bilateral, sagittal-plane, low-impact sport. The gym should supply what the bike does not:
- Single-leg strength and stability (the bike masks left/right imbalances).
- Posterior chain / hip-hinge strength (glutes, hamstrings) underused in the pedal stroke.
- Anti-extension and anti-rotation trunk strength for a stable platform and a healthy low back over long rides in flexion.
- Hip mobility (the antidote to hours in a fixed hip angle) and tissue quality.
- This is supportive work: it must never compromise the next key bike session.

## Coach-owned principles + default prescriptions

### 1. Train patterns, not body parts
Organize each gym day around: knee-dominant (squat), hip-dominant (hinge), push, pull, plus core woven between sets. For a cyclist, bias the lower-body slots toward single-leg variations.

### 2. Earn load with movement quality first ("don't add strength to dysfunction")
- Establish a clean body-weight squat pattern (femurs to parallel, weight on heels, sit back, minimal forward knee travel) before loading.
- Quick fix for depth limits: elevate heels ~2.5 cm to borrow ankle range while mobility improves.
- Never program half/quarter squats for "more weight" — higher injury risk, lower value.

### 3. Lead lower body with unilateral work (bilateral deficit)
An athlete is typically far stronger than half-bilateral on one leg, so single-leg work loads the legs hard while sparing the spine — ideal for a cyclist who wants leg strength without back risk.
- **Default progression:** split squat → rear-foot-elevated (RFE) split squat → single-leg squat. Start at the split squat as baseline.
- **Default sets/reps:** 3 sets x 6–8 reps/leg, RPE 7–8 (2–3 reps in reserve). Controlled tempo (~2–3 s down, no bounce). Rest 90–120 s between sets.
- Keep ~1 rep in reserve in any week that precedes a key ride.

### 4. Hip-dominant / posterior chain
- Trap-bar deadlift or kettlebell sumo deadlift = simpler-to-learn bilateral hinge when wanted.
- Single-leg straight-leg (modified) deadlift = the cyclist-friendly default for glute/hamstring and balance. Expect novel adductor-as-hip-extensor soreness early — ramp volume.
- **Default:** SL RDL 3 x 6–8/leg, RPE 7; hinge "deep hip movement, minimal knee bend," neutral spine.

### 5. Core = anti-movement, not crunches
Functionally the trunk muscles are stabilizers. Program the three anti- categories; do NOT program trunk twists, Russian twists, twisting sit-ups, or loaded lumbar rotation (lumbar rotation is small and best kept stable; rotation should come from the thoracic spine and hips).
- **Anti-extension:** plank → progressions; e.g. front plank 3 x 20–40 s, then rollouts/body-saws.
- **Anti-lateral-flexion:** side plank 3 x 20–40 s/side; loaded carries (suitcase/farmer).
- **Anti-rotation:** Pallof press / cable or band holds, 3 x 8–10 with 2–3 s holds, both sides.
- Place core between strength sets as active rest; it activates more than it fatigues.
- Cue breathing: full exhale to engage deep abdominals (diaphragm/abs antagonist pair) — "get tight" on the exhale.

### 6. Warm-up sequence (every session)
Foam roll → mobility/dynamic warm-up → power (if any) → strength.
- **Foam roll** the back side (calves, glutes, hamstrings, upper/lower back, adductors, TFL/hip rotators): seek tender spots, ~30–60 s per area. Combats "creep"/locked-long tissue from prolonged sitting and the cycling position. Roll the back side; do not also static-stretch it.
- **Dynamic warm-up:** treat split squats/lunges as sagittal hip-mobility drills, done in place for ~3 weeks before progressing to walking versions; backward lunge first (gentler on adductors).
- Drop the dated rotational warm-up drills (scorpion, dynamic trunk twists) — see Cautions.

### 7. Frequency & where strength sits in the week
- Off-season-style minimum for real adaptation: 2–3 strength days/week; 2 days forces compromises, 3 is the comfortable minimum. For an in-season cyclist, 2 quality days is realistic.
- Plyometrics (if used at all for a cyclist): do not load the same joints on consecutive days; avoid depth jumps. Low priority and high cost for a non-impact athlete — usually skip near key rides.

## Substitutions & progressions

- Body-weight loading ramp: wk1 3x8 → wk2 3x10 → wk3 3x12, then progress to a harder variation/load on wk4.
- Squat depth limited → heel-elevated body-weight/goblet squat to a box set to parallel.
- Can't yet single-leg squat → RFE split squat → split squat → assisted/elevated.
- No trap bar → kettlebell sumo deadlift or goblet squat for the hinge/knee slots.
- Anti-rotation without a cable → band Pallof, or offset (suitcase) carries.
- Plyometrics not appropriate → replace with extra single-leg strength or skip.

## Training/recovery cost + stop/downgrade rules (protect the next key ride)

- Single-leg and hinge work create meaningful leg soreness (especially early SL RDL adductor DOMS). Schedule these AFTER a key ride or with ≥48 h before one.
- The day before a key ride: no new lower-body strength stimulus, no plyometrics, no near-failure sets. Allowed: light mobility, foam rolling, easy core/breathing, easy carries.
- Downgrade rule: if legs are sore, sleep/HRV poor, or a hard ride is <48 h out → cut lower-body sets in half, cap RPE at 6–7, and keep ~2–3 reps in reserve, or move strength to a recovery day. Core/mobility can stay.
- Stop a set the moment squat/hinge pattern degrades — "strength on dysfunction" is how backs get hurt.
- Keep conditioning polarized: avoid grinding the gym in the 80–90% effort no-man's-land that just adds fatigue.

## Cautions (treat as hypotheses, not settled fact)

- Mechanism claims (foam rolling "why," creep/locked-long, breathing/PRI core model) are presented anecdotally; rolling likely helps tissue feel/readiness but the physiology is contested. Use it; don't over-claim.
- "Unilateral reduces injury and matches/beats bilateral performance" is largely the author's gym experience plus the bilateral-deficit literature — directionally useful, not proven for cyclists specifically.
- Blanket ban on lumbar rotation work is strongly stated; the anti-rotation emphasis is sound, but total avoidance is a conservative stance, not consensus.
- Book targets land-based, impact/striking team sports. Plyometrics and many power/agility drills are low-yield for a cyclist — adopt the strength/core/mobility chapters, largely ignore the plyo/agility prescriptions near training peaks.
- All set/rep/RPE numbers above are coach defaults layered on the book's principles, tuned down for a cyclist — they are not lifted from the text.
