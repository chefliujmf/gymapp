# Next Level (Sims & Yeager) — Female Masters / Peri-Menopause Source Notes

> **Applicability flag.** These notes cover FEMALE-athlete physiology with a
> PERIMENOPAUSE / MENOPAUSE (masters women) focus. They are knowledge for FUTURE
> female masters athletes of the coaching engine. **They do NOT apply to
> Jean-Manuel** (male athlete) and should never be applied to his plan or used
> to justify changes to it. Pull from this file only when coaching a
> peri/post-menopausal woman.

> **Source.** Inspired by *Next Level: Your Guide to Kicking Ass, Feeling Great,
> and Crushing Goals Through Menopause and Beyond* — Stacy T. Sims, PhD &
> Selene Yeager (Harmony/Rodale, 2022). Coach-owned paraphrase only; no
> transcription. Numbers cited are the book's prescriptions, restated.

> **Evidence caution — read first.** Sims is often strongly prescriptive and
> absolute in places where the underlying science is still emerging, mixed, or
> extrapolated from small studies. Treat the contested specifics flagged below
> as **HYPOTHESES to individualize and test**, not dogma. Always defer to the
> athlete's physician for anything hormonal, pharmaceutical, or clinical.

---

## 1. The core thesis (female masters)

- **What it covers.** As estrogen (E2) and progesterone fluctuate then decline,
  women lose the anabolic, bone-protective, vascular, thermoregulatory and
  sleep-regulating support those hormones provided. Training/fueling that worked
  pre-menopause stops working; the fix is to train *harder and shorter*, lift
  *heavy*, *jump*, eat *more protein* and *eat enough*, not to "slow down and
  eat less."
- **Coach principle.** For a peri/post-menopausal athlete, default toward:
  lower volume + higher intensity, year-round heavy lifting + plyometrics,
  deliberate protein dosing/timing, generous fueling around sessions, and
  proactive recovery (sleep, cooling, active recovery).
- **Caution / HYPOTHESIS.** The "sprint, don't slow down" framing is a useful
  corrective but not a license to ignore individual load tolerance, joint
  health, or training history. Build intensity progressively.

## 2. Cardio: dial volume down, intensity up (SIT/HIIT)

- **Relevance.** Female masters; substitution for chronic moderate "grey-zone"
  volume.
- **Coach principles + prescriptions.**
  - High intensity = HR above ~85% max, or RPE 9–10 on a 1–10 scale; recovery
    bouts at RPE 2–3.
  - Favor **SIT** (sprint interval training): very short, very hard efforts,
    ~10–30 s (40 s is the upper bound the book recommends).
  - **Dose:** off-season up to 3 SIT sessions/week with full recovery; 2/week
    is generally enough; in a busy/event-heavy block drop to 1/week to stay
    sharp. Keep **one truly long endurance session/week** (every ~10 days is
    enough for some).
  - **Cycling is often preferable to running** for hitting top intensities
    without joint/connective-tissue strain.
  - **Protocols (warm up 10–15 min first; cool down):**
    - *Tabata:* 20 s hard / 10 s easy ×6–8; recover 4–5 min; work up to 3 sets.
    - *40/20s:* 40 s hard / 20 s easy ×10; recover 5 min; up to 3 sets.
    - *30-on/30-off:* 30 s hard / 30 s easy ×4–6; recover 4 min; up to 3 sets.
    - *Hill repeats:* 20–30 s hard uphill, walk/jog down (lower-impact stimulus).
    - *Kettlebell SIT:* swings on a Tabata clock as an alternative to cardio gear.
- **Why (claimed).** Body-composition gains, visceral-fat loss, mitochondrial
  function, insulin sensitivity, VO2max, vascular responsiveness (fewer hot
  flashes), BDNF/cognition, and a net cortisol-lowering effect vs. chronic long
  endurance (which can keep cortisol elevated).
- **Caution / HYPOTHESIS.** Strong claims (e.g., specific % visceral-fat loss,
  "SIT beats running for menopausal women") come from small studies. Use as
  directional, individualize the dose, and watch recovery.

## 3. Strength: "lift heavy" year-round

- **Relevance.** Female masters; counters estrogen-driven loss of muscle satellite
  cells, strength, bone, posture, vascular health.
- **Coach principles + prescriptions.**
  - **Heavy = ~3–6 reps** at near-max load on **big compound lifts** (squat,
    deadlift, bench/press, rows) — spread load across large muscles, not single
    joints.
  - **Progression** (novice → heavy): start moderate, 2–3 sets × 8–15 reps for
    4–6 weeks (foundation), then 5×5, then 4–6 sets × 3–5 reps.
  - **Rest between heavy sets:** ≥2 min at 6 reps, up to 5 min for lower-rep
    heavier sets (this is strength work, not circuit/met-con).
  - **Frequency:** 2–3 days/week, **year-round** (not just off-season). In
    season, endurance athletes can pair LHS with SIT on the same day — **lift
    first** to potentiate, then intervals. Keep hardest lifting separate from
    longest endurance day.
  - Don't go light-and-high-rep ("toning"); that builds endurance, not the
    strength/satellite-cell stimulus masters women need. Light high-rep lifting
    is shown ineffective for endurance athletes' economy too.
  - Warm up thoroughly (foam roll + mobility, ~10–15 min) given higher joint
    pain/inflammation risk; learn/keep a braced neutral-spine setup.
- **Pelvic floor / "the canister."** Heavy lifting can provoke leakage; train
  the whole canister (pelvic floor + TrA + diaphragm + multifidus), not just
  Kegels. Kegels: 10 × 10-s holds + 10 short squeezes, daily. Add elevator abs
  and child's-pose diaphragmatic breathing. Plank "doming" = TrA not engaging;
  regress the plank.
- **Caution.** Osteoporosis/joint damage → clear with physician first; novices
  need coaching on load/technique before going heavy.

## 4. Plyometrics / jump training (bone + power)

- **Relevance.** Female masters; preserves fast-twitch power and is the strongest
  stimulus for bone (more than running).
- **Coach principles + prescriptions.**
  - **Dose:** ~10 min, 3×/week; easy to bolt onto the end of a strength session.
  - Bone study cited: jumping ~10–20×/day improved hip BMD over 16 weeks;
    20×/twice-daily beat 10×/twice-daily; both beat non-jumpers.
  - **Multi-directional impact matters** — running alone is too uni-directional.
    Especially important for cyclists/swimmers (non-impact sports).
  - **Progression:** master squat jumps first (8–10 reps, 1→2 sets) → beginner
    circuit (45 s on / 15 s off; jumping jacks, side hops, skipping; 1→3 rounds,
    5 min between) → intermediate (jump lunges, mountain climbers, sumo squat
    jumps) → advanced (tuck jumps/burpees, speed skaters, depth jumps, box jumps).
  - **Substitution for bad joints:** do the same patterns in shallow-end pool
    (aqua plyo) as HIIT; work with PT.
- **Caution.** Diagnosed osteoporosis → consult physician; phase in to condition
  connective tissue before volume.

## 5. Eat enough — Low Energy Availability (LEA) / RED-S

- **Relevance.** Female masters; the #1 problem the book sees. The menstrual
  "red flag" is gone in peri/post-menopause, so LEA/RED-S is harder to catch.
- **Coach principles.**
  - LEA = energy expenditure routinely exceeds intake → conservation mode:
    muscle breakdown, fat storage, lowered RMR, thyroid down-regulation, high
    cortisol, GH resistance, ↑ghrelin/↓leptin. Thyroid and bone formation can be
    disrupted after just ~4 days of low availability.
  - **Eating less + training more is wrong for active women** — it deepens LEA.
    Weight loss (if needed) happens *inside* adequate energy availability.
  - **Timing matters as much as total** — avoid "bookending" food around a bare
    middle; fuel in/around training. Don't under-eat on recovery days (the body
    is recovering).
- **Warning signs (often mislabeled "overtraining"):** stalled/declining
  performance & power, stress reactions/fractures, frequent illness, GI distress,
  poor coordination, chronic "hangriness," nocturnal hypoglycemia/waking.
- **Fix.** Shrink the intake-expenditure gap: ideally 3–4 weeks reduced formal
  training (or at least lower total volume, keep a few short hard sessions),
  increase fueling especially around workouts; rebuild gut microbiome with
  varied produce.
- **Diet-trap cautions (book is firm here):**
  - **Intermittent fasting** — discouraged for active women; female data show
    worsened insulin sensitivity in some, blunted autophagy, endocrine/thyroid
    suppression; effects amplified with exercise.
  - **Keto / very-low-carb (<50 g/day)** — discouraged for active women; impairs
    muscle fatigue resistance and bone-remodeling markers, raises cortisol,
    blunts kisspeptin.
  - **Plant-based (mostly plants + some quality animal food)** — endorsed for gut,
    inflammation, and possibly fewer vasomotor symptoms.
- **Caution / HYPOTHESIS.** The IF/keto "never for women" stance is stronger than
  the mixed literature warrants. Treat as a default-against, individualize; the
  *eat-enough* and *fuel-around-training* principles are the durable takeaways.

## 6. Macros & food quality for the transition

- **Relevance.** Female masters; more carb-sensitive, harder to hold muscle.
- **Baseline daily macros (g per kg bodyweight), before exercise fueling:**
  - *Sedentary day:* carbs ~2 g/kg; protein 1.8–2 g/kg (even distribution);
    fat ≥1 g/kg.
  - *Moderate day (a HIIT session or 1–2 h cardio — where most women land):*
    carbs ~3 g/kg; protein 2–2.2 g/kg; fat ~1.2 g/kg.
  - Activity tiers: light (walk/yoga/mobility) → moderate → heavy (two-a-days,
    >2.5 h hard cardio, or >4 h any cardio) scale energy up accordingly.
- **Protein (the headline number).** Aim **1.8–2.4 g/kg/day**, spread across
  3–4 evenly spaced meals each above the leucine threshold. Target ~30 g/meal,
  15–20 g/snack, and the biggest hit (**30–40 g**) right after training.
- **Leucine.** ~3–3.5 g leucine per feeding to hit the "leucine threshold" in
  peri/post-menopausal women (~0.4 g/kg protein per meal). MPS spikes then
  returns to baseline in ~2–3 h → distribution beats continuous grazing. Plant
  eaters need more total protein to hit threshold (~50 g soy ≈ 25 g whey).
- **Carb quality.** Most carbs from fruit/vegetables (fiber + phytonutrients +
  microbiome); save starchy carbs/whole grains for around training. Pair higher-
  GI fruit/veg with protein/fat. Substitute squash, sweet potato, beets,
  parsnips for bread/pasta. Maple syrup / honey > agave as sweeteners.
- **Cruciferous veg** (broccoli, Brussels, cauliflower, kale, cabbage) for
  estrogen-metabolism support (indole-3-carbinol → DIM). Roast to cut bitterness.
- **Protein powder:** convenience only; whey isolate best bioavailability,
  pea-isolate (+ hemp/brown rice) best vegan. Real food first.
- **Caution.** Macros are a *reference*, not a tracking mandate; the book prefers
  whole-food intuitive eating over calorie counting. High-protein + regular
  exercise carries low cancer risk (exercise modulates IGF-1).

## 7. Nutrient timing & fueling during exercise

- **Recovery window (female-specific claim):** eat within **~30 min** of any hard
  / >60–90 min session (women's window said to be shorter than men's) to exit the
  catabolic/high-cortisol state. 30–40 g protein (3–3.5 g leucine); the classic
  ~3:1 carb:protein still applies for glycogen + repair. Easy/yoga sessions →
  next meal is fine.
- **Pre-workout:** small carb+protein (banana + nut butter, toast + nut butter,
  dried fruit). Don't train fasted (the book treats fasted training as a net
  negative for masters women at any time of day).
- **During exercise (book avoids fixed g-carb/hr; uses cal/lb):**
  - ~**0.9–1.13 cal/lb/hr** for running/jostling activity (~140 cal/hr for a
    140-lb woman); ~**1.3–1.6 cal/lb/hr** for cycling/non-jostling (~225 cal/hr).
    When unsure, err low to avoid GI distress.
  - Prefer **mixed-macronutrient real food** over gels; menopausal women tolerate
    fructose-heavy products poorly (more circulating FFAs, GI "gut rot"). Sims
    does not recommend standard carb gels.
- **Caution / HYPOTHESIS.** The hard "30-min window, women shorter than men"
  claim is stronger than consensus. The robust, low-risk practice is: don't train
  fasted, and get protein + carb in reasonably soon after hard/long work.

## 8. Hydration (Sims' specialty; female + age differences)

- **Relevance.** Female masters; thirst becomes unreliable, hyponatremia risk.
- **Coach principles + prescriptions.**
  - Body fluid is never plain water — small sugar + sodium drive the gut's
    glucose-sodium co-transport. **Resting/low-intensity DIY drink:** 1 tsp maple
    syrup + 1/16 tsp salt in ~10 oz water (~4 g carb, ~150 mg sodium, ~1% sol.).
  - **Exercise drink target:** ~3–4% carb (glucose + sucrose, ~7–9.4 g/8 oz),
    sodium **~180–225 mg/8 oz** (≈360–450 mg per 16 oz), potassium ~60–75 mg/8 oz.
    Avoid the classic 5–8% carb sports drinks (GI distress, can dehydrate).
  - **Fluid rate:** ~0.12 oz/lb/hr at ≤75 °F (~17 oz/hr for 140 lb); ~0.16 oz/lb/hr
    at ~80 °F+ (~22 oz/hr). Smaller women less, larger more.
  - **Thirst is NOT reliable** for peri/post-menopausal women (hormones blunt
    thirst; postmenopausal women have lowest thirst drive). Use schedule, not
    thirst, when: 2+ heavy sessions/day; unacclimated to heat/altitude; history of
    heat illness. Drink to thirst is OK if prehydrated, heat-acclimated, well
    trained, on MHT, or with EAH/SIADH history.
  - **No salt tablets** — they stress the GI tract and can worsen dehydration;
    get sodium from drink + food (anchovies, smoked salmon, pickles, salted nuts).
  - **Prehydrate / hyperhydrate** before big/hot efforts with sodium-rich fluids
    (broth, miso, sodium preload mixes) — strong real-world payoff.
  - Avoid "light" drinks with sugar alcohols (sorbitol/mannitol/xylitol → GI) or
    sucralose (blood-sugar crash, possible insulin resistance with chronic use).
- **Heat / hot flashes during exercise:** ice-cold drink as internal heat sink;
  cool the neck/skin (ice in pantyhose down the jersey, cooling ties); layer
  clothing for the overheat→chill swing.

## 9. Sleep & recovery

- **Relevance.** Female masters; menopause wrecks sleep architecture
  (↓progesterone sedation, ↓estrogen REM/thermoregulation, ↓melatonin, hot
  flashes, nocturnal cortisol). ~61% of postmenopausal women report some insomnia.
- **Coach principles + prescriptions.**
  - Protect **7–9 h**; get to bed earlier to catch the GH window (most HGH
    secreted ~11 pm–1 am). Poor sleep → high cortisol, insulin resistance (one
    bad night ↓insulin sensitivity ~33%), poor glycogen restock, higher RPE.
  - **Sleep hygiene:** no alcohol within ~1 h of bed (wrecks REM, worsens hot
    flashes — switch to tart cherry juice); finish dinner 2–3 h before bed;
    screens off 30 min prior; bedroom ~65 °F + cooling sleepwear/sheets; a cool
    shower before bed; blackout + white noise; bedside pad for racing thoughts.
  - Evening exercise is OK (even sleep-improving) if it ends ≥1 h before bed.
    Caffeine: none after ~2 pm (6-h half-life).
  - **Stress down-shift:** diaphragmatic breathing (4-count in/out ×5–10), yoga,
    mindfulness/meditation (cited ~40% hot-flash reduction in one study).
  - **Sleep/recovery supplements (after adaptogens):** prefer valerian
    (400–600 mg) + Montmorency tart cherry juice (cold, 30 min pre-bed) over
    straight melatonin (use 0.3–1.0 mg if needed; watch hangover). Black cohosh
    only 4–6 months max (liver caution). Avoid sleeping pills.
  - **Wearables (Whoop/Oura):** good for trend-spotting and behavior feedback,
    but recovery scores are cardiovascular-strain-based — they miss muscular
    fatigue; don't treat a green score as clearance after heavy lifting.
- **Active recovery (female-specific notes):**
  - Always cool down 2–3 min (women drop arterial BP more post-exercise →
    wobbles/woozies; cool-down + cold recovery drink + compression help).
  - **Cold/ice baths help women** (need help vasoconstricting; opposite of the
    male caution) — counters post-exercise vasodilation.
  - On rest days do *truly easy* movement 45–60 min (masters women often do worse
    with total rest). Massage / massage guns, pneumatic compression (use within
    ~30 min for women), EMS, compression garments are all options.
- **Injury / immobilization recovery:** muscle loss is fast (~1 lb in 2 weeks,
  immobilized limb; strength lost ~3× faster than mass). Push protein to
  **0.9–1.1 g/lb/day** (≈126–154 g for 140 lb), 40 g × 4 meals (2–3 g leucine
  each) + 15–20 g snacks, every 3–5 h; whey for rapid leucine. Add ~4 g/day
  omega-3 (2 g am / 2 g pm). When in anabolic resistance, lead with protein —
  carbs don't speed synthesis then.

## 10. Symptom management while training

- **Hot flashes:** exercise doesn't reliably prevent them (may be more severe in
  very active women), but higher lean mass / lower body fat → fewer severe
  vasomotor symptoms. Manage with cooling + hydration + layering.
- **Periods/PMS (peri):** keep carbs for long hard work (10–15 g protein + 40 g
  carb pre-session >90 min; 40–50 g carb/hr during). Cramps: ~200 mg magnesium +
  ~1 g omega-3 + low-dose 80 mg aspirin in the week before. Plan for heavy/erratic
  bleeding (cups, period underwear, chamois). MHT can help erratic/heavy periods.
- **Headaches:** hydrate; nitrate-rich foods (beets, pomegranate, watermelon,
  spinach) for vasodilation.
- **Low mood / "the blahs":** raise circulating BCAAs/leucine (crosses BBB, blunts
  serotonin-driven CNS fatigue); manage stress.
- **Vaginal pain / GSM (affects cycling/running):** prescription vaginal estrogen
  or vaginal DHEA (local, low systemic effect; ACOG: vaginal estrogen doesn't
  raise breast-cancer risk) — physician-directed; OTC Replens/Revaree moisturizers.
- **Incontinence:** core/canister work + breathing; pelvic-floor PT if persistent;
  avoid bladder irritants (caffeine, spicy); protective wear meanwhile.
- **DOMS/aches:** adequate peri-workout protein/leucine; anti-inflammatory diet
  (fatty fish, berries, leafy greens, olive oil, nuts); curcumin may help.
- **Keep moving:** chronic sitting causes "exercise resistance" even in trained
  people — daily NEAT matters.

## 11. Bone health

- **Relevance.** Female masters; ~1 in 2 women >50 break a bone from osteoporosis;
  up to ~20% BMD loss in the first 5–7 years post-menopause (loss accelerates to
  ~2%/yr then settles ~1–1.5%/yr).
- **Coach principles + prescriptions.**
  - **Screen:** DEXA per NOF triggers (age ≥65; fracture after 50; menopausal +
    risk factors; height loss). T-score ≥ −1.0 normal; −1.1 to −2.4 osteopenia;
    ≤ −2.5 osteoporosis.
  - **Best stimuli:** weight-bearing + impact (jumping) + heavy resistance.
    Swimming/cycling alone are insufficient for bone → add impact + lifting.
  - Even diagnosed low-BMD women benefit from **heavy** lifting (LiftMOR study:
    deadlift/OHP/back squat, 5×5, 2×/week + impact chin-ups improved hip/spine
    BMD safely under supervision) — high-intensity beat low-intensity.
  - Posture/upper-back work to counter kyphosis.
  - **Nutrients (food first):** calcium 1,000 mg/<50, 1,200 mg/≥50 (from diet
    where possible; bolus supplements may carry CV risk); vitamin D; vitamin K
    (~100 mcg/day cut hip-fracture risk in Nurses' study); magnesium; K2.
  - **Bone robbers:** smoking, sedentariness, soda/cola, heavy alcohol, chronic
    stress, unresolved amenorrhea history.
- **Caution.** Diagnosed osteoporosis → physician clearance; may need to avoid
  high-impact/heavy-spinal-flexion-and-twist moves; supervised heavy lifting only.

## 12. Supplements — keepers vs. skips

- **Generally food-first; supplements largely unregulated.** Skip multivitamins,
  antioxidant supplements (can blunt training adaptation; beta-carotene harm in
  smokers), and over-the-counter compounded hormones.
- **Worth considering (individualize, physician where flagged):**
  - **Vitamin D** — check blood level; target ~75–100 nmol/L; keep supplement
    **≤4,000 IU**; >125 nmol/L can harm. Also suppresses hepcidin → aids iron.
  - **Iron** — common deficiency in women (worse newly-postmenopause due to
    exercise-elevated hepcidin); food first (heme iron + vitamin C); supplement
    may absorb better **every other day**; test before dosing. Needs: 18 mg/day
    ages 19–50, **8 mg/day after 50**.
  - **Magnesium** — ~320 mg/day baseline; heavy exercise raises need ~20%; helps
    sleep/mood/muscle; food first, supplement if active/symptomatic.
  - **Creatine** — notably useful for women (lower baseline stores). 3–5 g/day
    maintenance, or load 0.3 g/kg/day ×5–7 days; supports muscle/bone/strength
    with resistance training, may aid mood/cognition.
  - **Beta-alanine** — 4–6 g/day (split to avoid paresthesia) for competitive
    athletes; women respond well; may pre-empt hot flashes (vasodilation).
  - **Caffeine** — strong ergogenic; 2–6 mg/kg, ~45–60 min pre-effort; more
    benefit for sustained endurance than sprints in women; train with it first.
  - **DIM** — supports favorable estrogen-metabolite ratio. **Do not take with
    estrogen therapy** (reduces efficacy).
  - **Collagen** — jury out; possibly helps connective tissue taken ~1 h
    pre-exercise; low harm.
  - **BCAAs (fermented = vegan)** — prime MPS/brain, ease DOMS; do **not** replace
    whole protein post-workout.

## 13. Hormonal/clinical interventions (physician-led — context only)

> Coach stays in lane: surface options, never prescribe; route to physician.

- **MHT (menopausal hormone therapy).** Book reframes it as safer than the
  WHI-era scare suggested **when started within ~10 years of last period and
  before age 60**; transdermal may carry lower risk than oral. Good for hot
  flashes/night sweats, bone, possibly mood/sleep; **not** effective for building
  lean mass or (in already-fit women) reducing fat — exercise does that. Not
  risk-free (clots with oral, small breast-cancer signal with long use, dementia
  risk if started late). Bioidentical-from-compounding-pharmacy: cautioned against
  (unregulated dosing). On MHT, thirst regulation improves (can drink to thirst).
- **Non-hormonal Rx (for some symptoms):** SSRIs/SNRIs (mood + hot flashes),
  clonidine/lofexidine, fezolinetant (vasomotor); gabapentin works but Sims
  cautions it inflates HRV in female athletes (false recovery signal).
- **Adaptogens** (Sims is enthusiastic; "less is more," cycle them, food/organic
  source, check drug interactions). Coach-relevant doses as stated in book:
  - *Ashwagandha (calming):* 250 mg/day min (stress/anti-inflammatory), 300 mg
    ×2/day for menopause/body-comp/glucose; withanolides 5–8%. **Avoid if on
    thyroid meds.**
  - *Holy Basil (calming):* 500 mg ×2/day. **Avoid with anticoagulants.**
  - *Rhodiola (stimulating):* 150–600 mg/day (150 ×2). Early-day. Acts estrogenic
    — **avoid if estrogen is contraindicated**; avoid with MAOIs/immunosuppressants;
    can lower BP.
  - *Schisandra (stimulating):* 500 mg–2 g/day extract; min ~13.5 mg/kg/day. Not
    late-day (caffeine-like). Mild phytoestrogen.
  - *Maca (stimulating):* 2 g/day vasomotor, 3.5 g/day mood. **Avoid with thyroid
    meds; some products are WADA-banned** (drug-tested athletes beware).
- **Caution / HYPOTHESIS.** Adaptogen efficacy/dosing rests on small or
  traditional-use evidence; present as options to discuss with a physician,
  flag interactions, never as coach prescriptions.

## 14. Quick coaching checklist (female masters athlete)

1. Is she **eating enough**? Screen LEA/RED-S signs before assuming overtraining.
2. **Protein 1.8–2.4 g/kg/day**, ~30–40 g per feeding, 30–40 g post-session.
3. **Don't train fasted**; fuel before, refuel soon after.
4. Shift toward **lower volume / higher intensity** (SIT 1–3×/wk) + **one long day**.
5. **Heavy lifting 2–3×/wk year-round** (3–6 reps, compounds) + **plyo ~10 min ×3/wk**.
6. **Hydrate by schedule** in heat/doubles (thirst unreliable); sodium in-drink,
   no salt tabs; prehydrate big efforts.
7. Protect **sleep** (cooling, no late alcohol/caffeine, GH window) and program
   **real recovery**; cold baths OK for women.
8. **Bone:** impact + heavy lifting + vit D/K/Mg/Ca from food; DEXA per triggers.
9. Hormonal/clinical questions → **physician**; coach surfaces, never prescribes.
10. Treat every **flagged HYPOTHESIS** as something to individualize and monitor,
    not a rule.
