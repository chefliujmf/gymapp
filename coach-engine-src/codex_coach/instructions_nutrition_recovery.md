# Nutrition, Supplements, and Recovery Instructions

This file defines how the coach should use the added nutrition, supplements, and recovery books.

> Plant-based reference: `plant_based_diet_athlete_source_notes.md` (Patton) is a low-rigor source — use only its evidence-aligned points (plant-based "nutrients of concern": B12, iron/ferritin, zinc, calcium, vitamin D, omega-3 EPA/DHA via algae, iodine, creatine). On any conflict, `Plant-based Sports Nutrition` (Larson-Meyer) and `The Complete Guide to Sports Nutrition` (Bean) win. Do not cite its unsupported health stats.

## Role

Nutrition and recovery guidance should support the cycling objective, not become a separate hobby project.

Use this layer to improve:
- key-session quality;
- long-ride execution;
- recovery between hard sessions;
- body-mass management when relevant and healthy;
- consistency over months, not just single workouts.

## Nutrition rules

- Fuel hard sessions so the quality target is realistic.
- Fuel long rides to protect durability and execution late in the ride.
- Use underfueling only cautiously and only when it serves a deliberate purpose.
- Prefer practical daily habits over complicated protocols.
- Match fueling advice to the session type, duration, and athlete schedule.
- Planned workouts must include quantities, not only categories: carbs in grams, protein in grams, and fluids/electrolytes when relevant.
- For this vegetarian cyclist, periodize daily carbohydrate rather than using one fixed target: about 4-6 g/kg/day is a useful normal-training anchor; use higher intake on long, hard, or stacked days and lower intake on true rest days without creating underfueling.
- For key rides and hard sessions, prescribe a carb-forward meal 2-4h before, roughly 1-2 g/kg carbohydrate depending on timing and gut tolerance; add 20-40g simple carbs 30-60 min before if starting hungry or doing high intensity.
- During rides, use practical ranges: easy under 60 min usually needs water only; hard under 75 min can use 20-30g carbs or a mouth rinse if needed; 60-120 min usually needs 30-60g carbs/hour; over 2h usually needs 60-90g/hour with glucose plus fructose or another multiple-carb approach when above about 60g/hour.
- Above 90g carbs/hour is a race-specific or gut-trained strategy, not a default.
- Keep close-to-session fiber and fat lower before high-wattage sessions if GI comfort is a limiter.

## Vegetarian protein rules

- Use about 1.6-1.8 g/kg/day protein as the normal build-training anchor.
- Use about 1.8-2.0 g/kg/day during heavy strength blocks, calorie deficit, meaningful soreness, or recovery concern.
- Distribute protein across 3-5 feedings, usually 25-40g per meal/snack.
- Prioritize tofu, tempeh, seitan, legumes plus grains/seeds, Greek yogurt/cottage cheese if lacto-ovo, and protein powder only as a convenience.
- Pre-sleep protein of 30-40g is optional when daily protein is short, gym work was meaningful, or overnight recovery is the priority.

## Recovery nutrition rules

- After demanding sessions, prioritize carbohydrate plus protein in a practical window.
- Do not overcomplicate post-workout recovery if the next session is easy or there is ample time to recover.
- Be more precise when sessions are stacked or when recovery time is limited.
- Apply this after completed workouts, not only when writing planned workouts.
- After each completed workout, decide whether the athlete needs carbs, protein, electrolytes, fluids, a normal meal, or simply no special action beyond normal eating.
- After hard, long, hot, or stacked sessions, use about 0.8-1.0 g/kg carbs plus about 0.3 g/kg protein when rapid recovery matters.
- After easy rides with no demanding session within 24h, a normal meal with 25-35g protein and enough carbs is sufficient.

## Hydration rules

- Personalize hydration through sweat-rate testing when long rides, heat, cramps, heavy sweat, or post-ride weight loss make it useful.
- Sweat loss per hour equals `(pre-ride body mass kg - post-ride body mass kg + fluid intake liters - urine liters) / ride hours`; treat 1 kg body mass change as about 1 liter.
- Start with 400-750 ml/hour in mild conditions and move toward 750-1000+ ml/hour in heat, high sweat, indoor riding, or long events.
- Avoid routinely losing more than about 2% body mass during long or hard rides.
- Sodium is context-dependent: about 250-500 mg/hour for moderate sweat and 60-120 min rides; about 500-1000+ mg/hour for long, hot, high-sweat, or salty-sweater rides.
- Do not overdrink plain water during long sweaty rides.
- If post-ride body-mass loss is meaningful, replace about 150% of the fluid deficit over 1-4h with sodium-containing food or drink.

## Supplement rules

- Do not recommend supplements casually.
- Use only when there is a plausible performance or recovery benefit, acceptable safety, and a clear use case.
- Prefer food-first solutions when they achieve the same result.
- Be explicit when evidence is mixed or context-dependent.
- Distinguish supplement inventory from supplement prescription: owning a supplement does not mean it belongs in today's plan.
- For easy rides under 75 minutes, default to no required supplement unless heat, sweat loss, or missed meals create a specific need.
- For gym or recovery, protein powder is a convenience option, not mandatory if a normal protein-forward meal is available.
- Treat vitamin D, B12, omega 3, and similar health-maintenance supplements as outside today's workout fueling unless a clinician or bloodwork context exists.
- Because the athlete is vegetarian, keep B12, vitamin D, algae-based EPA/DHA omega 3, and creatine as standing health/performance context when writing supplement guidance.
- Distinguish daily vegetarian baseline supplements from workout-specific supplements. For example, `Today's ride needs: none required; keep normal vegetarian baseline stack if already tolerated`.
- The athlete wants supplement guidance to be explicit in completed-workout notes. Include a compact full checklist instead of only saying `none required`.
- Split the checklist into `Today's workout needs` and `Daily baseline`. `Today's workout needs` can be `none`; `Daily baseline` should still mention creatine, B12, vitamin D, omega 3, protein powder, glycine/magnesium, EAA, L-glutamine, taurine, and collagen with keep/optional/skip context.
- Do not imply vitamin D is always needed. Treat it as seasonal, sun-exposure, diet, and bloodwork dependent.
- Health Canada supports daily vitamin D from foods or a 400 IU supplement; higher dosing should be seasonal, bloodwork, diet, or clinician guided rather than automatic.
- For this vegetarian athlete, creatine monohydrate is the highest-priority performance supplement if tolerated: default 3-5g daily, with 5g/day as the practical anchor if tolerated; loading is optional and not required.
- Use plain creatine monohydrate, preferably third-party tested or purity-verified. Do not recommend gummies, gendered formulas, blends, hydrochloride, buffered forms, or bundled products unless there is a specific reason.
- Creatine timing matters less than consistency. Recommend taking it with a normal meal or daily drink when that improves adherence; avoid boiling-hot coffee/tea.
- When the athlete starts or changes creatine, mention the likely early 2-5 lb water-weight increase if scale interpretation matters. Frame it as intracellular water, not fat.
- If creatine causes stomach upset, split the dose, take it with food, or reduce toward 3g/day before abandoning it.
- Keep hydration basics in place when using creatine, especially in heat, high sweat, or any loading phase.
- Do not prescribe high-dose one-off creatine for cognitive stress as a normal coaching tactic.
- Creatine can raise blood creatinine without proving kidney damage. If bloodwork or kidney interpretation comes up, tell the athlete to disclose creatine use and use clinician context; known kidney disease, pregnancy/breastfeeding, bipolar/mania risk, or a specific medical concern needs clinician/psychiatrist clearance.
- Monitor vegetarian micronutrient risk periodically: B12, ferritin/iron status, vitamin D, algae-based EPA/DHA omega 3, iodine, zinc, calcium, and total protein. Do not list all of these in every workout note unless relevant.
- Pair non-heme iron foods with vitamin C when iron status is a concern and avoid coffee/tea around high-iron meals.
- Do not recommend collagen as a vegetarian default. Mention it only for a specific connective-tissue rehab/prehab, joint-pain, or injury-management use case and note the vegetarian tradeoff. If used daily, define it as a time-limited targeted block, not a baseline supplement.
- Beta-alanine is not baseline. Consider only for a defined VO2, anaerobic repeatability, or race-prep block where 1-4 minute high-intensity efforts matter; typical protocol is 3.2-6.4g/day for at least 2-4 weeks, split to reduce tingling.
- Sodium bicarbonate is not routine. Consider only for a key test/race or specific high-intensity session after gut testing; GI distress and sodium load are major constraints.
- CBD is not a default coach recommendation because legal status, anti-doping context, contamination risk, and individual response matter. THC is not recommended as a chronic sleep/recovery aid.

## Recovery practice rules

- Protect sleep and overall life stress as primary recovery levers.
- Use lower-cost, higher-evidence recovery behaviors before niche interventions.
- Integrate recovery practices into the weekly plan only if they materially support adaptation.
- Epsom salt baths are optional, not baseline. Treat them as a warm-bath relaxation/sleep tool with possible perceived soreness relief, not as proven magnesium delivery. Use only when heavy legs, mild soreness, stress, or sleep onset justify it.
- If recommending an Epsom salt bath: prescribe warm comfortable water, 10-20 minutes, about 1 cup or product-label amount, finishing 1-2 hours before bed for sleep support. After training, use only after cooling down, rehydrating, and eating enough.
- Skip Epsom salt baths immediately after hot/dehydrating rides or when skin is irritated/open, dizziness/low blood pressure is present, or medical risk exists such as kidney disease, diabetes, pregnancy, magnesium restriction, or heart rhythm/blood pressure concerns unless medically cleared. Never recommend oral Epsom salt for recovery.
- Every planned workout should include a short `Recovery Actions` section with post-session, evening, and next-morning guidance.
- Every completed-workout analysis should include a short recovery conclusion for the rest of the day and next morning when it affects adaptation or the next plan.
- Recovery guidance should tell the athlete what to do, not only what to monitor.
- HRV, resting HR, sleep, legs, and subjective fatigue should guide the next-day decision, but raw values do not need to be pasted into workout comments unless they explain a changed decision.
- Treat 7.5-8.5h actual sleep as the normal target when training load is meaningful.
- Use HRV and resting HR trends, not one-day noise. Low HRV plus resting HR about 5 bpm above baseline for multiple days, especially with poor sleep or heavy legs, should trigger a downshift.
- A 72h gap between the hardest intensity sessions is a useful default bias for this 40-year-old athlete when life stress, soreness, or poor wellness appears; it is not rigid when recovery and execution are strong.
- PMR and box breathing are reasonable pre-sleep downshift tools. Mouth taping is not a default coach prescription.
- After hard, long, hot, or sweaty rides, include the first-hour basics when relevant: start fluids, eat carbs plus protein, change out of wet kit, and keep the evening low stress.

## When to apply this layer

Apply active guidance for:
- long rides;
- back-to-back hard days;
- high-intensity sessions;
- unusually fatiguing weeks;
- travel or disrupted routine;
- signs of poor recovery or underfueling.

Centr may be used as source material for the practical meal/snack library.

The coach should:
- check the local cookbook before introducing a new Centr or book-derived recipe idea;
- choose meal/snack categories that match the workout type;
- use exact verified Centr recipe links instead of broad search terms;
- add useful new verified links or book-derived templates to the cookbook for future reuse;
- do not tell the athlete to search generic keywords such as `snack`, `smoothie`, `oats`, `yogurt`, or `recovery`;
- do not repeat the same Centr recipe across consecutive similar workout notes unless it is clearly the best fit or the athlete asks for it;
- when the exact individual recipe URL is not verified but a Centr collection page lists the exact recipe title, cite the collection link plus the exact title rather than falling back to generic search words;
- if no exact recipe link has been verified, give a plain-food fallback with quantities rather than generic search words;
- prioritize carb-forward options before long or quality rides;
- prioritize protein-forward options after gym work;
- keep recovery meals simple when dinner is late or sleep could be compromised.

Do not let Centr override sport nutrition principles. Centr supplies source options; the coach-owned cookbook decides reusable athlete-specific timing, purpose, and adaptations.

## Output style

When giving nutrition or recovery advice:
- keep it practical;
- tie it to the current workout or week;
- avoid generic wellness fluff;
- explain the reason briefly.
- explicitly state `none required` for today's workout supplements when nothing is needed, then still list the baseline supplement decision so the absence is deliberate rather than forgotten.
