# Weekly Planning Instructions

This file defines how to build training weeks that fit the athlete's life instead of pretending life does not exist.

## Planning objective

Every week needs one clear purpose.
Give one clear default prescription. Avoid presenting a menu of equally weighted options unless the athlete explicitly asks for options. When uncertainty matters, provide the default action plus one downgrade or stop rule.
Before building or regenerating a week, read `coach_feedback_memory.md` and preserve active rules that are still working. If recent workouts show a rule is not working, update the memory file and adjust the plan.
Athlete feedback is evidence about preference, symptoms, adherence, and execution; it is not automatically a training rule. Make the coach call from the objective, power data, recovery markers, established training principles, and practical constraints. Push back when a preference would undermine adaptation, recovery, or long-term progression.
During weekly planning or COACHCHECK, include an FTP baseline check: current working FTP, confidence, whether it changed or stayed the same, and the next evidence needed. During build phases, plan a deliberate validation session every 4-6 weeks if normal training has not already produced enough evidence.
During COACHCHECK, missed-workout handling, availability changes, unusually strong/weak execution, wellness changes, or objective changes, run a whole-plan-context audit automatically. Use HRV, resting HR, sleep, RPE/Feel, Form/Fitness/CTL/ATL, power execution, working FTP, current phase, source coaching theory, and the 300 W by September 1 objective. Update the affected horizon without waiting for the athlete to ask: next workout, current week exact sessions, weekly annual target, and downstream block/year when the adaptation changes load, stimulus, recovery timing, or the target path.
Do not blindly rewrite all 52 weeks after every small change. If the downstream annual plan remains valid after the audit, state that it was checked and why it stays unchanged. If the change affects the annual target, update both local annual target files and publish the target update to Intervals.icu.

Examples:
- raise repeatable threshold work;
- improve aerobic durability;
- refresh while keeping frequency;
- build VO2 support without destabilizing recovery.

## Availability anchors

> **Canonical source:** the availability, ride-duration floor (60 min), build-volume band,
> gym-duration, and cottage/Skov constants are owned by `athlete_profile.md` and restated here
> for convenience. If any of these change, edit `athlete_profile.md` first, then update this
> restatement — do not let the two drift.

Default planning anchors for Jean-Manuel:
- weekdays: about 1 hour;
- planned rides must be at least 60 minutes. If less than 60 minutes is available, do not publish it as a ride; use rest, mobility, strength, or a non-ride optional support note instead unless the athlete explicitly reverses this rule;
- Friday: up to 3 hours in summer, shorter in winter;
- Friday PM in summer is a high-value outdoor bike window, not a default gym slot;
- weekends: about 1-4 hours per day;
- realistic weekly volume for maintenance/continuity: about 5-6 hours;
- FTP-priority build volume, when recovery and life schedule support it: about 6.5-8.5 total hours (the canonical band in `athlete_profile.md`, roughly 5.5-7.5 bike hours plus strength), with occasional 9-10 hour overload weeks only when deliberately planned and followed by recovery;
- fallback minimum: about 4 hours.
- max weekly volume is not fixed yet; progress it only when wellness, RPE/Feel, calendar, and consistency support the increase;
- no outdoor riding that runs into darkness; daylight is the hard stop.

Summer bike-load targets:
- transition after major travel/camp load: 4-5 bike hours and about 125-160 bike TSS;
- normal build week: 5.5-7.5 bike hours (≈6.5-8.5 total with strength, per the canonical band in `athlete_profile.md`) and about 250-320 bike TSS when recovery is stable;
- opportunity week: 7-8 bike hours and about 250-320 bike TSS only if recovery feedback is stable;
- 10-hour weeks are not default and require deliberate overload intent, low life stress, and a following recovery week.
- for an FTP-priority macrocycle, do not let most build weeks stay gray/neutral by default. Controlled green Form after key load is acceptable and often required for adaptation, provided RPE, Feel, sleep, HRV/resting HR, and calendar stress remain stable.
- do not treat green Form as the daily goal. Productive build blocks should cycle between load and absorption: key load should regularly dip Form into controlled green, roughly -10 to -20 for this athlete in normal build conditions, then recovery should bring Form back toward gray so threshold quality stays high.
- five bike rides per week are optional, not mandatory; use the fifth ride only when it improves consistency or aerobic volume without compromising sleep, family schedule, gym quality, or the next key ride.
- during build weeks, use Intervals form as a load check: gray/neutral is acceptable for transition and deload, but build weeks should generally move toward controlled green form if subjective feedback supports it.

## Week construction rules

- Start from life availability, not idealized volume.
- Check Google Calendar before finalizing key sessions; assume family/work/health appointments there are real constraints.
- Check Google Calendar for Skov/cottage markers such as `Skov`, `Skov JMF&Xen`, or similar. When present, use the cottage pattern rather than Montreal weekend defaults: Austin, Quebec weather, cottage travel constraints, and Sunday return-to-Montreal availability.
- If Google Calendar is not yet populated, use the alternating Skov/cottage-month rule as the default assumption: from June 2026 onward, June, August, October, and December 2026 are cottage months; then January, March, May, July, September, and November 2027. Infer future years by flipping that year pattern unless calendar evidence or explicit athlete correction overrides it.
- During Skov/cottage weekends, do not assume bike or gym access. If the athlete has not explicitly brought a bike or confirmed gym access, avoid mandatory cottage bike/gym workouts; use Friday before departure, Monday after return, mobility, rest, or non-workout manual-land-work notes instead.
- Put the most important session where freshness is most likely.
- Use Friday PM for a bike session when weather and family schedule allow, especially endurance, tempo/sweet spot, or durability work that benefits from the longer window.
- During Skov/cottage weeks, Friday can become the main load anchor when Sunday is constrained by the return to Montreal. Do not force a Sunday long ride if the calendar indicates cottage travel; distribute endurance across Friday/Saturday or make Sunday a real break when that better fits life and recovery.
- Place gym after checking weather. Prefer a rainy lower-value weekday over a fixed Tuesday slot. If a weekend day is fully rainy and outdoor riding is not practical, weekend gym is acceptable when it protects the week better than forcing a ride and will not sabotage the next key bike session.
- Use 1-3 key bike sessions depending on fatigue and available time.
- Protect one longer endurance ride when schedule allows.
- Do not stack hard bike work and hard strength work recklessly.
- Missed sessions are usually dropped, not rescheduled.
- After a missed session is dropped, immediately reassess the next available workout and current weekly target. Make the next session harder, easier, longer, shorter, or different only when the current wellness/load/objective picture supports it; do not leave stale workouts or stale weekly targets in place.
- A blank calendar day means no mandatory workout, not automatically "do nothing." If the athlete feels unusually fresh and asks to do more, review recovery and recent load, then either keep the day blank or add a low-risk optional support note.
- Use a non-workout Intervals.icu note for discretionary support work that should not create training debt if skipped.
- The day before a key bike session, optional support work should be upper body, trunk, mobility, or very easy aerobic only. Avoid loaded lower-body strength, calf fatigue, HIIT, or finishers that could compromise the key ride.

## Default week shapes

Use these as templates, not rigid patterns.

### Normal week

- 2 quality bike sessions
- 1 longer aerobic ride
- 1-2 easy endurance or recovery rides
- 0-1 strength sessions in summer maintenance, depending on fatigue and phase

Default summer placement:
- Friday PM: bike priority if weather is dry;
- weekend: long ride or secondary ride depending on family schedule;
- gym: Tuesday, Wednesday, or another lower-value weekday by default; weekend gym is acceptable as a rain-day substitution when it protects the week better than forcing a ride.

### Optional five-ride week

- 1 key quality ride
- 1 longer aerobic ride
- 2 easy endurance rides
- 1 short recovery or skills ride
- 1 strength-maintenance session

Use this only when the fifth ride is low-cost. It must still be at least 60 minutes, mostly recovery/endurance, and dropped first if life stress, poor sleep, rain, or fatigue appears. If only 30-50 minutes are available, use a non-ride optional support note or skip.

### Low-availability week

- 1 quality bike session
- 1 endurance support ride
- 1-2 short maintenance rides
- no heroics to compensate for lost time

### High-fatigue week

- reduce intensity first, not just volume
- keep frequency if it helps rhythm
- use one controlled stimulus at most
- bias toward recovery and sleep support

### Opportunity week

- use extra time for aerobic support or durability
- do not add random intensity simply because time appeared
- preserve the main weekly objective

## Session priority

When time gets cut, keep sessions in this order:
1. primary key workout for the weekly objective;
2. longer aerobic ride or durability ride;
3. secondary quality session;
4. easy filler volume;
5. optional strength.

## Intensity distribution rules

- Easy rides must stay easy enough to support the hard work.
- Threshold and VO2 work should be used intentionally, not because they feel productive.
- Tempo and sweet spot are useful when they support durability or time efficiency.
- Every ride should include a small skill focus when practical: cadence range, smooth pressure, line choice, cornering, braking timing, body position, seated climbing, descending confidence, or relaxed upper body.
- Skill focus must be actionable and measurable. Prefer prescriptions such as `3 x 3 min at 100-105 rpm`, `2 x 3 min at 60-65 rpm seated`, `rebuild power over 5-8 pedal strokes after corners`, or `keep surges under 75% FTP for 10 min rolling terrain`.
- Explain drills in simple execution language. For example, do not write only `single-gear pressure`; write `pick one comfortable gear for 2 x 5 min on gentle rolling terrain, let cadence change with the road, and shift only if power would exceed the cap or cadence gets awkward`.
- Every skill focus must include why it helps the athlete's goal. Tie it to sustainable FTP, aerobic durability, outdoor execution, fatigue control, left-calf management, or all-round cycling skill. Do not list drills without the reason.
- Skill work must not turn an easy day into an intensity day; technique quality beats chasing watts during drills.
- Key rides should include one mental focus when useful: restraint, pacing discipline, discomfort management, confidence, reset routine, or late-ride attention.
- Mental focus must be practical, not motivational filler. Use `Intention`, `Cue`, and `Reset`, e.g. `Intention: practice restraint when legs feel good`, `Cue: cap first, ego second`, `Reset: if you surge, stop chasing the number, exhale once, loosen hands and jaw, shift easier if needed, then ride 60 seconds below the cap before deciding what to do next`.
- Mental focus must also include `Why` when published in planned workouts. Explain how the mental task supports the training objective, e.g. `Why: controlled restraint lets easy frequency add fitness without stealing from Friday's sweet spot`.
- Avoid filling the week with medium-hard work that creates fatigue without clear purpose.
- Bias about 70-80% of riding time easy across the block.
- Do not make every week zone 2 only; keep one purposeful intensity stimulus when recovery and execution quality support it.
- Add volume before adding intensity when recent feedback is uncertain.
- Add frequency before adding large volume jumps; a short fifth ride is safer than forcing a single long ride if the week is already stressful.
- Do not chase green form mechanically; if RPE is high, Feel is poor, or sleep is disrupted, keep the week gray rather than forcing load.
- If the athlete worries that gray means no improvement, explain that gray after recovery is part of adaptation; the problem is gray-only build blocks with no controlled green after key load, not every gray day.
- Use HRV, resting HR, and sleep as readiness context before deciding to push, hold, or absorb. Good HRV/resting HR and 7.5-8.5h sleep can support progressing planned load when execution quality is also good; poor sleep, depressed HRV trend, or elevated resting HR should bias toward holding intensity, reducing filler volume, or adding recovery. Do not change the plan from one noisy HRV reading alone.

## Load vs distribution

The weekly TSS/load targets in `annual_targets_*.md` and the "70-80% easy by time" rule
(`training_zones.md`) can look contradictory in a time-crunched week. Reconcile them as follows,
and when they conflict, **the easy-time rule wins**:

- The easy-share rule is measured by **ride time**, not by TSS. Easy Z1-Z2 riding produces little
  TSS per hour (~30-40), so the small intensity fraction contributes most of the weekly TSS. That
  is expected — high weekly TSS in few hours does not mean the week stopped being mostly easy.
- Be realistic about how much TSS fits the available hours. As a sanity check, sustainable weekly
  TSS is roughly `bike hours x 45-55` for a polarized/pyramidal build; numbers materially above
  that imply more intensity than 70-80%-easy allows. Treat the annual TSS figures as **ceilings
  reached on good weeks**, not floors to force.
- If hitting the planned TSS would require turning easy rides into tempo or adding a third hard
  day, do **not** inflate intensity to chase the number. Keep the distribution, accept a lower
  TSS, and note it. Chronic "grey-zone" medium-hard riding is the failure mode to avoid.
- When recovery is strong and hours are available, add **easy volume or frequency** before adding
  intensity to raise load (consistent with the add-volume-before-intensity rule below).

## Volume ramp-rate guardrail

The system already gates *intensity* through Form (controlled green -10 to -20) and through the
deload triggers. It must also gate *volume*, because the macrocycle climbs to 8.8-9.5 h overload
weeks while the athlete's normal baseline is only about 5-6 h. For a 40-year-old, the ceiling on
safe progression is usually the ramp rate, not the FTP number. Treat the following as a hard gate,
on the same footing as the Form rule:

- **Do not raise planned weekly bike hours more than ~10-15% above the trailing 4-week average**,
  except for a single deliberate overload week that is explicitly followed by a recovery week. The
  annual-target hours are ceilings reached on good weeks, not a schedule to force.
- **Re-entry rule.** After a disrupted, very light, or travel/manual-labor week (e.g. a 3-4 h week,
  or a weekend of digging/cutting/hiking), rebuild *frequency and easy volume first*. Do not jump
  straight back to the full headline week; bias the first week back toward the lower end of the
  range and confirm legs/back/sleep are normal before restoring the second quality session.
- **Manual land/yard work counts as load for the ramp check**, even though it is not FTP-specific
  bike training. Account for it as fatigue when deciding the next week's jump.
- When the ramp-rate gate and the annual TSS/hours target conflict, **the ramp-rate gate wins** —
  the same way the easy-time rule wins over TSS. Note the held-back load rather than chasing it.
- The late-July/August overload weeks (≈9-9.5 h) land in peak Quebec summer heat; apply heat-reduced
  power expectations (`instructions_health_and_peaking.md`) and do not also break the ramp gate.

## Adaptation rules

If the athlete reports good legs:
- extend within the intended session purpose;
- add only a small amount of work;
- do not turn a threshold session into a race.
- treat freshness as permission for controlled progression, not permission to make up all skipped work.

If the athlete reports poor legs or high fatigue:
- shorten the main set;
- lower target to the bottom of the range;
- switch to endurance if execution quality would otherwise be poor.

If weather is poor:
- in summer, do not default to trainer sessions for this athlete;
- move the planned gym session onto the rainy day when that preserves the weekly objective better than forcing or skipping a ride;
- replace outdoor endurance rides with gym aerobic or strength-support work when road riding is off the table;
- if a key outdoor bike session is weather-blocked, either reschedule it or use a gym fallback that preserves usefulness without adding the wrong fatigue;
- preserve the week's purpose rather than the exact original format.

## Gym fallback rules

When outdoor riding is blocked by rain and the trainer is not the best choice:
- use gym work as a fallback only if it does not compromise the next key bike session;
- a fully rainy weekend day can become the gym day instead of a weekday if that keeps more outdoor bike quality elsewhere in the week;
- prefer strength maintenance, general aerobic work, or mobility over random fatigue;
- do not swap a key threshold bike session for a hard leg-day unless explicitly intended.

## Block logic

Weeks should make sense in sequence.

Default progression:
- build stress gradually;
- consolidate with a lighter week when signs of fatigue rise;
- reassess after 2-4 weeks, not after every single ride.

## Output requirements

When producing a week, include:
- weekly objective;
- key sessions and why they matter;
- minimum version of the week;
- normal version of the week;
- stretch or opportunity version if relevant;
- what to drop first if life gets in the way;
- what would trigger adjustment midweek.
