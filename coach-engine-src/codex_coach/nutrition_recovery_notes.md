## Vegetarian Athlete Defaults

The athlete is vegetarian. Treat vegetarian-relevant supplements as daily health-maintenance context, not as today's workout fueling unless the session has a specific need.

Default daily stack to consider:
- creatine monohydrate: reasonable daily support for a vegetarian cyclist doing strength and repeatability work; use plain monohydrate, preferably third-party tested or purity-verified, and plan it deliberately rather than adding randomly before rides;
- B12: routine priority unless diet reliably includes enough eggs, dairy, or fortified foods;
- vitamin D: likely useful in Canada, especially fall/winter or if bloodwork is low; keep dose conservative unless guided by labs;
- omega 3: prefer algae-based EPA/DHA if no fish intake; flax/chia/walnuts are useful ALA sources but not the same as direct EPA/DHA;
- protein powder: convenience only when normal vegetarian food will not cover protein around training or recovery;
- collagen: not a vegetarian default and not a general daily recommendation; consider only for a specific connective-tissue rehab/prehab or joint-pain goal and discuss the vegetarian tradeoff first.

Monitor before adding more:
- ferritin/CBC for iron status, especially with endurance load;
- iodine intake, especially if dairy/eggs/iodized salt are low;
- calcium and total protein intake from daily diet.

## Practical Supplement Bias

Default:
- no supplement is required for easy rides under 75 minutes;
- protein powder is useful only when normal food will not cover protein around gym or recovery meals;
- glycine with magnesium before bed can remain part of the sleep routine if already tolerated;
- vitamin D, B12, and omega 3 should be treated as vegetarian-relevant health-maintenance supplements, ideally guided by diet, bloodwork, or clinician advice;
- creatine can be considered for strength and repeatability blocks, but should be planned as a consistent daily baseline rather than added casually before rides;
- collagen is only relevant when connective-tissue support is a specific goal, usually paired with rehab/prehab timing or a defined daily injury-management block, and it is not vegetarian;
- EAA is usually redundant if total protein intake is adequate;
- L-glutamine and taurine should not be default training recommendations without a specific reason.

## Completed-Workout Supplement Checklist

The athlete wants the supplement line in coach notes to feel complete, not forgotten. For completed-workout notes, use a compact checklist:

- Today's workout needs: caffeine, electrolytes/sodium, carbs, protein powder, or none required.
- Daily baseline to keep if already chosen and tolerated: creatine; B12; algae-based omega 3; vitamin D only when season, sun exposure, diet, or bloodwork support it; glycine with magnesium before bed only if it supports sleep.
- Convenience only: protein powder when a normal protein-forward meal is not practical.
- Usually skip unless a specific reason exists: EAA, L-glutamine, taurine.
- Collagen: not a vegetarian default; mention only for a specific connective-tissue prehab/rehab, joint-pain, or injury-management discussion and call out the vegetarian tradeoff.

## Creatine Source Update

Local source notes:
- [creatine_source_notes.md](</Users/jmfiset/dev/cyclingcoach/knowledge_base/creatine_source_notes.md>)
- source PDF: [How to Take Creatine_ A Doctor’s Practical Guide..pdf](</Users/jmfiset/dev/cyclingcoach/knowledge_base/How to Take Creatine_ A Doctor’s Practical Guide..pdf>)

Coach interpretation:
- the PDF supports the current vegetarian baseline rule: creatine monohydrate is the highest-priority performance supplement for this athlete if tolerated;
- use plain creatine monohydrate, preferably third-party tested or purity-verified;
- keep the coach default at 3-5 g/day, with 5 g/day as the practical anchor if tolerated;
- loading is optional and not required. It can saturate stores faster, but routine coaching should not require it;
- timing matters less than consistency. Take it with a normal meal or daily drink; avoid boiling-hot coffee/tea;
- expect a possible 2-5 lb early body-mass increase from water held in muscle, not fat;
- split the dose, take with food, or reduce toward 3 g/day if stomach upset appears;
- keep hydration basics in place, especially in heat or during any loading phase;
- disclose creatine use before kidney-related bloodwork interpretation because creatinine may rise without proving kidney damage;
- use clinician/psychiatrist clearance for known kidney disease, pregnancy/breastfeeding, bipolar/mania risk, or a relevant medical concern.

## Epsom Salt Bath Source Update

Local source notes:
- [epsom_salt_bath_source_notes.md](</Users/jmfiset/dev/cyclingcoach/knowledge_base/epsom_salt_bath_source_notes.md>)

Evidence summary:
- Epsom salt is magnesium sulfate, but current evidence does not support treating an Epsom salt bath as reliable magnesium supplementation.
- The more defensible benefit is the warm bath itself: relaxation, reduced perceived muscle tension, and sleep support when timed well.
- Warm baths or showers have better sleep-support evidence when used 1-2 hours before bedtime for at least 10 minutes.

Coach rule:
- optional recovery practice, not a daily baseline supplement;
- use after demanding rides, gym sessions, heavy legs, or stressful evenings when the target is relaxation, perceived soreness relief, or better sleep;
- for sleep: 10-20 minutes, warm but not scalding, finishing 1-2 hours before bed;
- for post-workout soreness: same evening or first few hours after training only after cooling down, rehydrating, and eating enough;
- skip immediately after hot/dehydrating rides, before driving if it causes drowsiness, or with open wounds, skin infection, severe skin irritation, dizziness/low blood pressure symptoms, kidney disease, diabetes, pregnancy, or magnesium restriction unless medically cleared;
- do not ingest Epsom salt for recovery or "detox."

## Vegetarian Cyclist Deep Research Update

Local source notes:
- [vegetarian_cyclist_performance_source_notes.md](</Users/jmfiset/dev/cyclingcoach/knowledge_base/vegetarian_cyclist_performance_source_notes.md>)
- source PDF: [Vegetarian Cyclist Performance Optimization Plan.pdf](</Users/jmfiset/dev/cyclingcoach/knowledge_base/Vegetarian Cyclist Performance Optimization Plan.pdf>)

Coach interpretation:
- use the PDF as a useful checklist, not as an automatic protocol;
- adopt the stronger rules on carbohydrate periodization, vegetarian protein sufficiency, sweat-rate-based hydration, creatine, B12/iron/vitamin D/omega 3 monitoring, sleep, HRV/RHR trend checks, and desk-job mobility;
- keep weaker or higher-risk items cautious: beta-alanine only for defined high-intensity blocks, sodium bicarbonate only after gut testing for key efforts, CBD only with legal/anti-doping/product-quality caution, and no THC as a chronic sleep aid;
- do not default to high-dose vitamin D, mouth taping, K2, cannabinoids, or supplement-heavy recovery.

Operational anchors:
- daily protein: about 1.6-1.8 g/kg/day in normal build training, 1.8-2.0 g/kg/day during heavy strength, calorie deficit, or recovery concern;
- protein distribution: roughly 25-40 g per meal/snack across 3-5 feedings;
- daily carbs: periodize around training load, commonly 4-6 g/kg/day in normal training and higher on long/hard days;
- key-session pre-fuel: about 1-2 g/kg carbs 2-4h before, plus optional 20-40 g simple carbs 30-60 min before;
- ride carbs: usually none for easy rides under 60 min, 30-60 g/h for 60-120 min, and 60-90 g/h for rides over 2h when gut tolerance supports it;
- hydration: personalize with sweat-rate testing and pair long/hot/high-sweat rides with sodium rather than plain water only;
- creatine: if tolerated, 3-5 g/day is the vegetarian baseline performance supplement, with 5 g/day as the practical anchor; loading is optional.
