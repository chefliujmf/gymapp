# Plant Based Diet for Athlete — Source Notes (cross-checked)

## Source
- "PLANT BASED DIET FOR ATHLETE: The Complete Guide" — Howard Patton MD (2021), .azw3 in `knowledge_base/`.
- Extracted text is low quality (homoglyph/OCR artefacts). Used only as inspiration, not transcription (repo copyright policy).
- **Quality verdict: LOW-RIGOR popular source.** ~80% of the book is ethics / environmental / animal-welfare advocacy and vegetarian history, not sports nutrition. The athlete-specific nutrition content is thin, generic, and in places inaccurate. The recipe section is internally inconsistent (a "plant-based" book that includes recipes with honey and wild salmon).
- **Authoritative references in this knowledge base supersede this book on every nutrition number:** `Plant-based Sports Nutrition` (Larson-Meyer & Ruscigno) and `The Complete Guide to Sports Nutrition` (Bean). Defer to those.

## What it covers
- History of vegetarianism/veganism; types of vegetarian diet (lacto-ovo, lacto, ovo, vegan, raw).
- Animal-welfare and environmental arguments for plant-based eating (the bulk of the book).
- A short "benefits/risks for athletes" section.
- A list of nutrients plant-based athletes must plan for.
- A recipe appendix (smoothies, tofu scrambles, grain bowls, tempeh dishes).

## Relevance
- Jean-Manuel is vegetarian (lacto-ovo by current profile), so the *nutrient-planning checklist* is the only genuinely relevant takeaway, and even that is better sourced elsewhere.
- Use this file mainly as a CAUTION map: it documents what NOT to act on from this book and confirms the standard plant-based "nutrients of concern" list, which the coach already tracks.

## Coach-owned principles (evidence-aligned only)
The book is correct that a well-planned plant-based diet is adequate for athletes and that a handful of nutrients need deliberate planning. The book does NOT give usable dosing; the numbers below come from the authoritative references, not this book.

- Nutrients of concern for a plant-based/vegetarian athlete (the one durable takeaway):
  - **Vitamin B12** — no reliable plant source; supplement or fortified foods are mandatory. (Book states this correctly.)
  - **Iron** — plant (non-heme) iron is less bioavailable; pair iron-rich plants with vitamin C; monitor ferritin, especially given endurance training and historically higher needs in athletes.
  - **Zinc** — phytate reduces absorption; legumes, nuts, seeds, whole grains; soaking/sprouting/fermenting helps.
  - **Calcium** — fortified plant milks, tofu set with calcium, leafy greens (low-oxalate), tahini.
  - **Vitamin D** — supplement per status/season (low sun = supplement); test if uncertain.
  - **Omega-3 (EPA/DHA)** — ALA from flax/chia/walnut converts poorly; an algae-based EPA/DHA supplement is the cleanest vegetarian route. (Book flags DHA as a gap; correct.)
  - **Iodine** — book mentions it in passing; worth tracking (iodized salt or seaweed in moderation).
  - **Creatine** — lower in vegetarians because dietary creatine is animal-sourced; a standard creatine monohydrate supplement is well-supported for power/strength and is one of the highest-value supplements for a vegetarian athlete. (Book lists creatine as a gap; correct.)
- Protein: the book only says "vegetarians can get enough protein if variety/amount are adequate" and correctly notes that strict per-meal protein *combining* is unnecessary across a balanced day. It gives NO athlete target. Use the authoritative target: roughly **1.4–2.0 g/kg/day** for an endurance/strength-training athlete, spread across meals, with attention to leucine-rich sources (soy/tofu/tempeh/edamame, legumes, seitan, plus dairy/eggs for a lacto-ovo athlete). Confirm against Bean / Larson-Meyer.
- Food-quality framing the book gets right: a vegetarian diet is not automatically healthy — refined carbs, sugar, fried/junk "vegan" foods can still wreck a diet. Emphasize whole foods, legumes, whole grains, fruit/veg, nuts/seeds.
- Vitamin C + iron pairing (e.g., citrus/tomato/pepper with beans/lentils/fortified cereal) is a legitimate, low-effort absorption tactic worth prescribing.

## Concrete prescriptions (carry over only what's defensible)
- Keep B12, vitamin D, omega-3 (algae or fish-oil per the athlete's choice), and creatine as standing vegetarian health-maintenance items (already in the supplement inventory).
- Monitor iron status (ferritin) periodically rather than blanket-supplementing iron; over-supplementing iron is a real risk.
- Build the iron+vitamin C pairing into everyday meal guidance.
- Useful practical food ideas from the recipe section (concepts, not recipes): tofu scramble with turmeric for breakfast protein; grain bowls (quinoa/brown rice + beans + greens + sweet potato + seeds) as a recovery-friendly template; tempeh as a high-protein, fermented (better-absorbed zinc/iron) staple; hemp/chia/pumpkin seeds as easy protein + ALA + mineral toppers; pea/soy protein powder in post-ride smoothies.

## Substitutions / progressions
- Lacto-ovo athlete (Jean-Manuel): eggs and dairy simplify protein, B12, calcium, and leucine intake versus full vegan — lean on them where convenient.
- Transition pacing: the book's one reasonable behavioral point is that gradual dietary change sticks better and reduces GI upset; apply to any future diet shifts.
- Plant milks/yogurts: choose **fortified** (calcium + B12 + D) versions to passively cover micros.

## Cautions — claims in THIS book to NOT act on
- **No usable sports-nutrition numbers.** It gives no protein g/kg, no carb fueling targets, no hydration math, no peri-workout timing. Do not treat its absence of guidance as guidance.
- **Exaggerated/uncited health stats.** E.g., "red meat increases heart disease risk by 1,000%", "three rashers of bacon a day = +20% cancer risk", diabetes "reversal" claims, "60% more" diabetes reduction. These are cherry-picked or distorted; do not cite as fact. (The WHO red/processed-meat classification is real but the book overstates magnitudes.)
- **"Improved mood / serotonin / arachidonic acid" causal claims** are oversimplified and not robust; ignore for coaching.
- **Beauty/skin/cosmetic-absorption claims** ("60% of products absorbed into bloodstream", dairy→acne as settled fact) are off-topic and unreliable.
- **Environmental/animal-welfare statistics** dominate the book and may be motivating but are not coaching inputs and are not individually verified here — do not present as established figures.
- **Recipe inconsistencies:** several "plant-based" recipes include honey and wild salmon. If used, treat as recipe inspiration only and adapt to the athlete's actual diet; do not rely on the book for dietary-consistency logic.
- **No "alkaline/detox" pseudoscience** was prominent (good), but the general "natural = healthier" framing should not override evidence-based supplement decisions (e.g., creatine, algae omega-3 are processed supplements and are exactly what a vegetarian athlete should use).
- **Bottom line:** when this book and the authoritative references disagree, the authoritative references win. Use this source only to confirm the standard plant-based nutrients-of-concern checklist.
