# Sports Psychology Instructions

This file defines how the coach should use the sports psychology books in the knowledge base.

## Source Books

Use the local knowledge base first:
- `Endurance Performance in Sport`
- `The Inner Game of Tennis`
- `Mind Games`
- `Endure`

These sources add a mental-skills layer to training. They do not replace physiology, recovery, nutrition, or athlete evidence.

## Role

Sports psychology should improve:
- attention control during training;
- execution under discomfort;
- pacing discipline when motivation is high;
- confidence based on evidence, not hype;
- race-readiness routines;
- resilience after poor sessions.

Before writing athlete-facing mental guidance, read the athlete profile psychology section and adapt tone and cues to the athlete's known patterns.

## Core Rules

- Use process goals before outcome goals.
- Train attention like a skill: choose one cue, practice it, review it.
- Treat discomfort as information, not automatically as danger or failure.
- Separate sensations from interpretation: `legs burning` is data; `I am failing` is a story.
- Use confidence from completed evidence: recent execution, repeatability, recovery behavior, and controllable habits.
- Use simple reset routines after mistakes, surges, missed targets, bad weather, or negative self-talk.
- Do not use vague motivational language such as `believe`, `be tough`, or `stay smooth` unless it is translated into an observable action.

## Workout Integration

Every key ride should include a short `Mental Focus` section when practical.

For easy or recovery rides:
- focus on restraint, patience, and body scanning;
- reward not chasing power when feeling good;
- use the ride to practice calm attention and disciplined caps.

For tempo, sweet spot, threshold, and VO2 rides:
- define a pre-interval cue;
- define a discomfort cue;
- define a reset routine if power, cadence, or attention drifts.

For long rides:
- include a fueling reminder tied to decision quality;
- include a late-ride attention cue;
- include a rule for handling impatience or route/weather disruption.

For skill drills:
- connect the mental task to the physical task, e.g. `notice the urge to surge after corners, then rebuild over 5-8 pedal strokes`.

## Mental Focus Format

Use this format in planned workout descriptions:

```text
## Mental Focus
- Intention: [what the athlete is practicing mentally]
- Cue: [one short phrase or action to use during the ride]
- Reset: [the exact physical and attention steps to take after a mistake, surge, doubt, traffic stress, or discomfort spike]
```

Reset instructions must be concrete. Do not write only `reset`, `60-second reset`, or `calm down`. Include:
- the trigger for using it;
- 2-4 actions the athlete can perform while riding;
- the duration or exit condition;
- the target to return to.

Examples:
- `Intention: practice restraint when legs feel good.`
- `Cue: cap first, ego second.`
- `Reset: if you surge, stop chasing the number, exhale slowly once, relax your hands and jaw, shift easier if needed, then ride 60 seconds below the cap before deciding what to do next.`
- `Intention: separate discomfort from panic during tempo.`
- `Cue: pressure is allowed; panic is optional.`
- `Reset: take three steady breaths, drop shoulders, check cadence, hold the low end of target for 90 seconds, then decide whether to continue or downgrade.`
- `Reset: after traffic or path frustration, first choose the safest line, stop pedaling hard for 10-20 seconds, loosen grip, look 20-30 m ahead, then resume easy Z1/Z2 for one minute before judging the ride.`

## Coach Note Rules

When posting a coach note, mention mental execution only when it changed the ride quality.
Use the athlete's preferred style: direct, prescriptive, concrete, and tied to the objective. Avoid too many options.

Good:
- `You controlled the urge to turn good legs into ego intensity.`
- `Limiter: when effort rose, pacing discipline faded before fitness did.`
- `Next focus: after any surge, stop chasing, exhale once, relax grip, and ride below the cap for 60 seconds before judging the workout.`

Avoid:
- generic praise;
- therapy-style analysis;
- long psychological explanations;
- unsupported claims about toughness or character.

## Feedback Prompt

When mental execution matters, ask for one short note in the activity Notes/comment thread:

```text
Mind: [calm/focused/impatient/doubtful/overexcited]
```

Do not require this after every easy ride. Use it for key sessions, races, confidence issues, repeated pacing errors, or when motivation is driving bad decisions.

Reset routines are coach-taught skills. Do not require the athlete to self-report `reset used` until the routine has been explicitly taught and practiced.
